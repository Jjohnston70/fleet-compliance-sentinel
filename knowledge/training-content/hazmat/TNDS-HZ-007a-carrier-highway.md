# TNDS-HZ-007a: Carrier Requirements -- Highway

## Learning Objectives

After completing this module, you will be able to:

- Describe the dual regulatory framework (PHMSA + FMCSA) governing hazmat highway transport
- Apply loading and unloading safety procedures correctly
- Read and apply the hazmat segregation table for mixed loads
- Explain attendance and surveillance requirements for parked hazmat vehicles
- Identify parking distance restrictions and safe haven requirements
- Follow hazmat routing rules and tunnel/bridge restrictions
- Respond correctly to tire failures, fires, and emergencies on a hazmat vehicle
- Describe driver responsibilities during a hazmat incident

---

## 1. Highway Carrier Responsibilities

When you drive a truck carrying hazardous materials on public highways, you operate under two overlapping sets of federal regulations:

- **PHMSA (Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration)** regulations under 49 CFR Parts 171-180 govern the hazardous material itself -- packaging, labeling, placarding, and shipping papers.
- **FMCSA (Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration)** regulations under 49 CFR Parts 177 and 397 govern how you transport those materials -- loading procedures, driving rules, parking, routing, and emergency response.

**The driver is the last line of defense.** The shipper classified the material. The packaging manufacturer built the container. The warehouse loaded it. But you are the person who takes it out on the highway, and you are the person who must deal with it if something goes wrong at 65 mph.

### Driver Responsibilities Before Departure

1. **Inspect shipping papers.** Verify that every hazmat shipment on your vehicle has complete, legible shipping papers with the proper shipping name, hazard class, UN/NA number, packing group, and emergency contact number. The papers must be within immediate reach while driving (on the seat or in the door pouch) and on the driver's seat or in the door holder when you leave the cab.

2. **Verify placards match cargo.** Walk around the vehicle and confirm that the displayed placards match the hazard classes shown on the shipping papers. If the placards are wrong, do not leave until they are corrected.

3. **Check packaging integrity.** Look for leaking containers, damaged drums, broken seals, crushed boxes, or packages that are not oriented correctly (arrows must point up). If a package is damaged and leaking, do not transport it. Notify your dispatcher and the shipper.

4. **Know your emergency procedures.** You must have access to the Emergency Response Guidebook (ERG) or equivalent emergency response information for every hazmat load. Know the guide number for your cargo before you start driving.

> **Real-World Scenario:** A driver picks up a load of mixed chemicals at a distribution warehouse. The warehouse dock worker hands over the shipping papers listing three different hazardous materials. The driver notices the truck has FLAMMABLE placards from a previous load, but one of the current materials is a Class 8 Corrosive. The driver stops, requests CORROSIVE placards, and does not leave the facility until all four sides are correctly placarded. This is exactly what the regulations require.

## 2. Loading and Unloading Rules (49 CFR 177.834-842)

Loading and unloading are the highest-risk moments in hazmat transport. The material is being moved, opened, transferred, or exposed. The regulations under Part 177 establish strict procedures.

### General Requirements for All Hazmat

- **Set the parking brake.** Before any loading or unloading begins, the parking brake must be set. The vehicle must not move during the operation.
- **Turn off the engine.** The engine must be off during loading and unloading. Exception: when the engine is needed to operate a pump for transferring product (e.g., fuel delivery), the engine may run, but only for the pump operation.
- **No smoking within 25 feet.** No person may smoke within 25 feet of any vehicle being loaded or unloaded with hazmat. This includes e-cigarettes and any open flame.
- **Package orientation.** Packages marked with orientation arrows ("THIS SIDE UP") must be loaded with arrows pointing upward. Containers designed for upright storage must be stored upright.
- **No damaged packages.** Do not load any package that is leaking, has a broken seal, shows damage to the outer container, or appears to have been improperly packaged. Report it to the shipper and your dispatcher.
- **Secure the cargo.** All packages must be braced, blocked, or otherwise secured to prevent shifting or falling during transport. A 55-gallon drum of solvent that topples over and breaks open at a highway speed event is a HAZMAT incident.

### Flammable Liquids -- Grounding and Bonding

When transferring flammable liquids between a cargo tank and a storage tank (fuel delivery, for example):

- **Grounding:** The cargo tank must be electrically grounded to the earth to prevent static charge buildup.
- **Bonding:** A bonding wire must connect the cargo tank to the receiving tank so both are at the same electrical potential.
- **Why it matters:** Static electricity can ignite flammable vapors. A single spark during fuel transfer can cause a fire or explosion. Grounding and bonding eliminate the voltage difference that causes sparks.

> **Scenario:** A fuel delivery driver pulls up to a gas station. Before opening any valves, the driver connects the grounding cable from the truck to the grounding lug at the station, then connects the bonding cable between the truck's discharge port and the station's fill port. Only after both connections are made does the driver begin the transfer. This is not optional -- it is the law, and it prevents fires.

### Specific Loading Rules by Material

- **Explosives (Division 1.1-1.6):** Must be loaded in a vehicle with a closed body or covered with a tarpaulin. No metal tools near detonators. No detonators loaded in the same vehicle with other explosives (with limited exceptions).
- **Flammable solids and oxidizers:** Keep away from heat sources. Do not load with acids.
- **Corrosives:** Load below other freight to prevent leaks from dripping onto other cargo. Secure upright.
- **Poisons:** Never load in the same vehicle space with foodstuffs, animal feed, or any material intended for consumption by humans or animals.

## 3. Segregation and Separation (49 CFR 177.848)

Not all hazardous materials can ride together. Some combinations react violently. The segregation table in 49 CFR 177.848 tells you which materials can share a vehicle and which cannot.

### Reading the Segregation Table

The table is a grid with hazard classes listed across the top and down the side. Where a row and column intersect, you will find one of these symbols:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **X** | **Do not load together in the same vehicle.** These materials are incompatible. They must be in separate vehicles. |
| **O** | **May be loaded together only if separated by a non-combustible divider.** The divider must be solid, non-combustible (metal or equivalent), and must physically separate the two materials within the vehicle. |
| *(blank)* | **No segregation restriction.** These materials may be loaded together. |

### Critical Combinations to Know

These are the combinations that cause the most serious incidents when the segregation rules are violated:

1. **Oxidizers + Flammable Liquids = X (do not load together).** Oxidizers feed fire. Flammable liquids are fuel. If a container of each breaks open in the same vehicle space, you have the two ingredients for an intense fire or explosion.

2. **Poisons + Foodstuffs = Absolute prohibition.** Poisonous materials (Division 6.1) must never be loaded in the same vehicle with food, feed, or any edible material. This is not just a segregation table issue -- it is a separate, specific prohibition in 49 CFR 177.841(e). Contamination of food with toxic chemicals is a public health catastrophe.

3. **Cyanides + Acids = Produces hydrogen cyanide gas.** This is a lethal combination. Cyanide salts reacting with even mild acids produce hydrogen cyanide, a fast-acting lethal gas. These materials must be separated.

4. **Oxidizers + Organic Peroxides.** Organic peroxides are inherently unstable. Adding an oxidizer accelerates decomposition. Do not load together.

> **Worked Example:** A dispatcher assigns a driver to pick up three shipments:
>
> - Shipment A: 500 lbs of sodium hypochlorite solution (Division 5.1, Oxidizer)
> - Shipment B: 300 lbs of acetone (Division 3, Flammable Liquid)
> - Shipment C: 200 lbs of sodium hydroxide solution (Class 8, Corrosive)
>
> The driver checks the segregation table:
> - Oxidizer (5.1) vs. Flammable Liquid (3) = **X** (do not load together)
>
> The driver cannot carry Shipment A and Shipment B in the same vehicle. The driver contacts dispatch to arrange separate transport or sequence the deliveries so both are never on the truck at the same time.
>
> - Oxidizer (5.1) vs. Corrosive (8) = **blank** (no restriction)
> - Flammable Liquid (3) vs. Corrosive (8) = **blank** (no restriction)
>
> The driver could carry A + C together, or B + C together, but not all three.

## 4. Attendance and Surveillance (49 CFR 397.5)

A vehicle containing hazardous materials must be attended or parked in a safe haven at all times when it is on a public road or shoulder.

### What "Attended" Means

A hazmat vehicle is "attended" when the driver (or a qualified representative):

- Is **within 100 feet** of the vehicle
- Has an **unobstructed view** of the vehicle
- Is **awake and alert** (not sleeping in the sleeper berth)
- Is **aware** that the vehicle contains hazmat and knows what to do in an emergency

Sitting in a restaurant 200 feet from the truck does not count. Sleeping in the cab while parked at a truck stop does not count. Standing at a fuel island 50 feet away with a clear line of sight to the vehicle does count.

### What a "Safe Haven" Is

A safe haven is a location approved for the parking of unattended vehicles carrying hazmat. This includes:

- The property of the cargo's **consignee** (the delivery destination)
- The **carrier's own terminal**
- A location designated by government authorities or approved for hazmat vehicle parking

A random truck stop, a highway shoulder, or a Walmart parking lot is **not** a safe haven unless specifically designated as one.

> **Scenario:** A driver hauling chlorine (Division 2.3, Poison Gas) needs to use the restroom at a truck stop. The restroom is inside the building, 250 feet from the truck. The driver cannot leave the vehicle unattended at a truck stop -- it is not a safe haven. Options:
>
> 1. Use a facility where the restroom is within 100 feet and the driver can maintain an unobstructed view.
> 2. Have a co-driver or qualified person stay with the vehicle.
> 3. Park at a designated safe haven before leaving the vehicle.
>
> This is one of the most practically challenging rules for solo hazmat drivers, and it requires planning stops in advance.

## 5. Parking Rules (49 CFR 397.7)

When a hazmat vehicle must be parked, the location matters. These rules protect the public from the consequences of a hazmat release at a stationary vehicle.

### General Parking Rules (All Hazmat)

- **Never park within 5 feet of the traveled portion of the road.** The vehicle must be completely off the roadway, not partially on the shoulder with the wheels in the traffic lane.
- **Never park within 300 feet** of a bridge, tunnel, dwelling, building, or place where people gather (church, school, stadium, theater) **unless there is no practicable alternative.** If you must park within 300 feet due to an operational necessity (e.g., making a delivery to that building), that is permitted, but you do not park near these structures for convenience, rest, or meals.

### Explosives Parking Rules (Division 1.1, 1.2, 1.3)

Vehicles carrying Division 1.1, 1.2, or 1.3 explosives have the strictest parking rules:

- Must comply with the **300-foot rule** from all structures, plus:
- Must be parked in a location approved by local authorities when possible
- Must be attended at all times or parked in a safe haven

> **Scenario:** A driver hauling Division 5.1 Oxidizer needs to stop for the night. The driver sees a truck stop with available parking. The nearest building (the truck stop convenience store) is 150 feet from the available parking spaces. Can the driver park there?
>
> Under 49 CFR 397.7(a), the driver should not park within 300 feet of a building unless there is no practicable alternative. If another safe parking location exists farther from buildings, the driver must use it. If this is the only available option after a reasonable search, the driver may park there but should document the necessity.

## 6. Routing (49 CFR 397.67, Subpart C)

Hazmat vehicles cannot always take the shortest or fastest route. Federal and state regulations may require specific routing.

### Designated Routes

Many states and municipalities designate specific routes for hazmat vehicles, particularly for:

- **Radioactive materials (Highway Route Controlled Quantities):** Must use Interstate highways or state-designated alternate routes whenever possible. Drivers must use the route that results in the lowest overall radiation exposure to the public.
- **Other hazmat:** States and cities may designate routes through industrial areas and away from residential neighborhoods, hospitals, and schools.

### When No Designated Route Exists

If there is no designated route for the material being transported, the driver must use the route that **minimizes time through or near populated areas.** This does not always mean the shortest route or the fastest route. It means the route with the least exposure of the public to risk.

> **Practical Application:** A driver delivering flammable liquids has two route options from the terminal to the customer:
>
> - Route A: 15 miles through a downtown area with schools, hospitals, and heavy pedestrian traffic. Estimated time: 40 minutes.
> - Route B: 22 miles on a bypass highway through an industrial zone. Estimated time: 35 minutes.
>
> Route B is the correct choice. It is longer in distance but passes through fewer populated areas and actually takes less time. Even if Route B took 45 minutes, it would still be the better choice because it minimizes exposure to populated areas.

### Know Your Routes

Drivers should familiarize themselves with hazmat routing in their regular operating area. Many carriers post approved hazmat routes in their terminals and include them in route planning software. If you are entering an unfamiliar area, contact dispatch or check with local authorities before proceeding.

## 7. Fires, Smoking, and Fueling (49 CFR 397.11-15)

### Open Fires

- **Do not drive a hazmat vehicle near an open fire** unless you can safely pass without stopping. If a fire is blocking or adjacent to the road, stop at a safe distance and do not proceed until the fire is out or you receive clearance from emergency personnel.
- **Do not park within 300 feet of an open fire.** If a fire starts near your parked vehicle, move the vehicle immediately if it is safe to do so.

### Smoking

- **No smoking within 25 feet of a hazmat vehicle.** This applies whether the vehicle is loaded with hazmat or is empty but has not been cleaned of hazmat residue.
- This rule applies to the driver, co-driver, dock workers, bystanders -- everyone. The driver is responsible for enforcing this around the vehicle.
- "No smoking" means no cigarettes, no cigars, no pipes, no e-cigarettes, no matches, no lighters, no open flames of any kind.

> **Common Violation:** A driver parks a tanker carrying gasoline residue (the tank is "empty" but not cleaned) at a truck stop, walks 30 feet away, and lights a cigarette. Another driver is smoking 15 feet from the tanker. Both are in violation. The tanker contains flammable vapors even though the liquid has been unloaded. Vapors are what ignite, not the liquid itself.

### Fueling

- **Engine off during fueling** in most cases. The engine may remain running only when it is necessary to operate equipment during the fueling process (rare).
- **Someone must be at the nozzle** at all times during fueling. Do not insert the nozzle, lock it open, and walk away. A fuel spill on a hazmat vehicle creates an additional hazard.

## 8. Tire and Vehicle Inspection (49 CFR 397.17)

Tire failures on hazmat vehicles are not just inconveniences -- they are potential ignition sources and safety hazards.

### Inspection Requirements

- **At the beginning of each trip:** Inspect all tires before departure.
- **Each time the vehicle is parked:** Inspect tires when you stop. Feel for heat. Look for damage, bulges, cuts, or low pressure.
- **During the trip:** Be alert for handling changes that may indicate a tire problem (vibration, pulling to one side).

### What to Do About a Problem Tire

- **Flat tire or hot tire:** Do not continue driving. A flat tire running on the rim generates sparks. A hot tire may be about to blow out or catch fire. Either condition near a cargo of flammable or explosive material is extremely dangerous.
- **Immediate action:** Move the vehicle to a safe location away from populated areas if possible. Remove the damaged tire or wait for roadside service. Do not drive on a damaged tire to "get to the next exit."

> **Scenario:** A driver hauling Division 1.5 blasting agents feels a vibration and pulls over to check. The right rear outside tire is flat and hot. The driver:
>
> 1. Parks the vehicle as far from the road and any structures as possible.
> 2. Sets the parking brake and chocks the wheels.
> 3. Does NOT attempt to change the tire (the hot tire could ignite if disturbed near the cargo).
> 4. Calls for roadside service and notifies dispatch.
> 5. Remains with the vehicle (attendance requirement) and keeps bystanders away.
>
> This is the correct response. The instinct to "just change it and keep going" can lead to a fire or worse when hazmat is on board.

## 9. Emergency Response and Incident Reporting

When something goes wrong with a hazmat load on the highway, the driver's actions in the first minutes determine whether the situation is contained or becomes a disaster.

### Driver's Immediate Actions (in order)

1. **Protect yourself.** Move upwind and uphill from any spill or release. Do not walk through spilled material. Do not inhale vapors.
2. **Warn others.** Keep bystanders, other motorists, and pedestrians away from the area. Use emergency triangles or flares (but NOT flares near flammable materials -- use triangles only).
3. **Isolate the area.** Establish a perimeter. The ERG provides recommended isolation distances for each material.
4. **Call for help.** Call 911. Then call your carrier's emergency number and the emergency contact number listed on the shipping papers.
5. **Provide information.** Give the shipping papers to emergency responders when they arrive. The papers tell responders exactly what material is involved, how much there is, and what hazards it presents.

### Using the ERG

The Emergency Response Guidebook (ERG 2024) is your field reference. Every hazmat driver should carry a copy and know how to use it:

1. Look up the four-digit UN/NA number in the yellow-bordered pages (numerical index) or the material name in the blue-bordered pages (alphabetical index).
2. The index gives you a three-digit Guide Number.
3. Turn to that Guide Number in the orange-bordered pages for specific emergency response guidance: fire, spill, and first aid procedures.
4. If the material is highlighted in the index, check the green-bordered pages for initial isolation and protective action distances.

### Incident Reporting

- **Immediate notification:** Certain hazmat releases require immediate telephone notification to the National Response Center (1-800-424-8802). This includes: death or major injury, evacuation of the general public, closure of a major roadway or facility, or release of a marine pollutant exceeding its reportable quantity.
- **Written report:** A detailed written Hazardous Materials Incident Report (DOT Form 5800.1) must be filed within 30 days of any incident involving unintentional release of hazmat during transport, or any event that meets the reporting criteria above.

> **Scenario:** A driver is involved in a rear-end collision. The impact cracks a drum of hydrochloric acid (Class 8, Corrosive, UN 1789) in the trailer. The driver smells acid and sees liquid dripping from the trailer.
>
> 1. The driver exits the cab, moves upwind, and does not approach the leaking drum.
> 2. The driver warns nearby motorists to stay back and move upwind.
> 3. The driver calls 911, reports a hazmat spill, and provides the material name, UN number, and hazard class.
> 4. The driver calls the carrier's emergency number.
> 5. When fire department HAZMAT team arrives, the driver hands over the shipping papers.
> 6. After the scene is cleared, the driver notifies the National Response Center.
> 7. Within 30 days, the carrier files DOT Form 5800.1.

## 10. Tunnels and Bridges

Many tunnels and some bridges restrict or completely prohibit hazmat vehicles. These restrictions exist because a hazmat incident in an enclosed tunnel or on a bridge over water presents extreme danger with limited escape routes and difficult emergency access.

### What Drivers Must Know

- **Plan your route in advance.** Know which tunnels and bridges along your route allow hazmat and which do not.
- **Restrictions vary by material class.** Some tunnels ban all hazmat. Others ban specific classes (e.g., explosives and flammable gases) but allow corrosives and non-flammable materials.
- **Time-of-day restrictions.** Some facilities allow hazmat passage only during off-peak hours (e.g., between 10:00 PM and 6:00 AM) to reduce public exposure.
- **Escort requirements.** Some tunnels require hazmat vehicles to be escorted through by facility personnel. The driver must follow escort instructions exactly.
- **Placards trigger enforcement.** Tunnel and bridge authorities identify hazmat vehicles by their placards. This is another reason why removing placards from empty vehicles matters -- an unplacarded empty truck can use tunnels and bridges freely; a placarded empty truck may be turned away.

### Common Examples

- **Lincoln Tunnel, Holland Tunnel (New York/New Jersey):** Significant hazmat restrictions. Many classes prohibited entirely. Hazmat vehicles are often routed to the George Washington Bridge instead.
- **Fort McHenry Tunnel, Baltimore Harbor Tunnel (Maryland):** Restrict various hazmat classes. The Key Bridge (prior to 2024) or alternate routes must be used.
- **Eisenhower Tunnel (Colorado, I-70):** Restricts many hazmat classes due to elevation and limited ventilation.

> **Practical Tip:** When planning a route through an unfamiliar area, check tunnel and bridge restrictions before you start driving. Your carrier's route planning department, state DOT websites, and commercial GPS systems with hazmat routing features can identify restricted tunnels and bridges. Do not assume that because a tunnel is on a major interstate, it permits hazmat. Many do not.

### Q1
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: According to the segregation table in 49 CFR 177.848, what does an "X" at the intersection of two hazard classes mean?
**A**: The materials may be loaded together without restriction
**B**: The materials may be loaded together if separated by a non-combustible divider
**C**: The materials must not be loaded in the same vehicle (correct)
**D**: The materials require special permits to be loaded together
**Explanation**: An "X" in the segregation table means the two materials are incompatible and must not be loaded, transported, or stored together in the same vehicle. They require completely separate transport. An "O" means they may share a vehicle only with a non-combustible divider between them.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 177.848(d)

### Q2
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A driver hauling hazardous materials parks the vehicle at a truck stop and walks 150 feet away to enter the restaurant, leaving the vehicle unattended. The truck stop is not a designated safe haven. Is this a violation?
**A**: No, because truck stops are automatically considered safe havens
**B**: Yes, because the driver is beyond 100 feet from the vehicle and the location is not a safe haven (correct)
**C**: No, because the driver can still see the vehicle from inside the restaurant
**D**: Yes, but only if the vehicle is carrying explosives
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 397.5, a hazmat vehicle on a public road must be attended at all times or parked in a safe haven. "Attended" requires the driver to be within 100 feet with an unobstructed view. At 150 feet, the driver exceeds the 100-foot limit. A truck stop is not a safe haven unless specifically designated as one.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 397.5(a)

**A**: No closer than 100 feet
**B**: No closer than 300 feet, unless there is no practicable alternative (correct)
**C**: No closer than 500 feet under any circumstances
**D**: There are no parking distance restrictions for hazmat vehicles

The "no practicable alternative" exception covers operational necessities like making a delivery, but does not apply to convenience stops.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 397.7(a)

**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 177.834(c), 49 CFR 397.13

### Q5
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A driver discovers a hot tire on a vehicle carrying flammable liquids during a trip inspection. What should the driver do?
**A**: Continue driving slowly to the nearest service station
**B**: Stop immediately in a safe location, do not continue driving on the damaged tire, and call for service (correct)
**C**: Deflate the tire and continue driving on the rim to the next exit
**D**: Ignore it if the tire is not visibly flat
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 397.17, a driver who discovers a tire that is flat, leaking, or overheated on a hazmat vehicle must stop at the nearest safe location. A hot tire on a vehicle carrying flammable liquids is a fire hazard -- sparks from a rim or a tire fire could ignite the cargo. The driver must not continue driving on the damaged tire.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 397.17(b)

### Q6
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: Which of the following cargo combinations would violate the hazmat segregation rules?
**A**: Corrosive material loaded with non-flammable gas
**B**: Oxidizer loaded with flammable liquid in the same vehicle (correct)
**C**: Flammable solid loaded with corrosive material
**D**: Non-flammable gas loaded with Class 9 miscellaneous material
**Explanation**: The segregation table in 49 CFR 177.848 shows an "X" at the intersection of Division 5.1 (Oxidizer) and Division 3 (Flammable Liquid), meaning they must not be loaded together. Oxidizers support combustion, and flammable liquids are fuel -- combining them in the same vehicle creates a severe fire and explosion risk if containers are breached.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 177.848(d)

### Q7
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: When no designated hazmat route exists, what routing principle must a driver follow?
**A**: Always take the shortest route to minimize time the vehicle is on the road
**B**: Always use the interstate highway system regardless of distance
**C**: Use the route that minimizes time through or near populated areas (correct)
**D**: Follow the same route used for non-hazmat loads
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 397.67, when no state or local designated route exists, carriers and drivers must use the route that reduces the risk to the public. This means choosing routes that minimize time spent traveling through populated areas, even if those routes are longer in distance.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 397.67

### Q8
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A hazmat vehicle is involved in a crash that causes a drum of toxic material to rupture and spill onto the highway. After ensuring personal safety and warning bystanders, what is the driver's NEXT priority?
**A**: Begin cleaning up the spill using materials from the truck
**B**: Attempt to patch the leaking drum
**C**: Contact 911, then call the carrier emergency number, and provide shipping papers to responders when they arrive (correct)
**D**: Drive the vehicle off the highway to prevent traffic delays
**Explanation**: After protecting themselves and warning others, the driver's next actions are to call for professional help (911 and the carrier's emergency line) and provide responders with the shipping papers that identify the material. Drivers should not attempt to clean up hazmat spills or handle damaged containers -- that is the job of trained HAZMAT response teams. Moving a damaged vehicle could worsen the release.
