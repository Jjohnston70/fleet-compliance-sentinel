# TNDS-HZ-005: Labeling Requirements

## Learning Objectives

After completing this module, you will be able to:

- Describe the purpose, shape, and minimum size of hazmat labels
- Identify each of the nine DOT hazard class labels by color and symbol
- Explain the difference between primary and subsidiary labels and when both are required
- Apply correct label placement rules to packages
- Recognize situations where labeling exceptions apply
- Connect package-level labels to vehicle-level placard requirements

---

## Section 1: What Are Hazmat Labels?

Hazmat labels are **diamond-shaped hazard indicators** placed on individual packages to communicate the specific type of danger the material inside presents. They are the visual warning system that tells anyone who handles, loads, stores, or responds to an incident involving that package exactly what kind of hazard they face.

### Physical Specifications

- **Shape:** Square-on-point (diamond orientation), rotated 45 degrees
- **Minimum size:** Each side must be at least **100 mm (3.9 inches)** long
- **Border:** Solid line set 5 mm (0.2 inches) inside the edge of the label
- **Design elements:** Each label has a specific combination of background color, a graphic symbol in the upper half, the hazard class number in the bottom corner, and in some cases descriptive text
- **Material:** Must be durable enough to withstand exposure to weather and handling without fading, peeling, or becoming illegible during transport

### Labels vs. Placards: Same Design, Different Scale

Labels and placards use the same visual system -- same colors, same symbols, same hazard class numbers. The difference is scale and location:

| Feature | Label | Placard |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Size | 100 mm (3.9 in) per side | 273 mm (10.75 in) per side |
| Goes on | Individual packages | Transport vehicle (four sides) |
| Placed by | Shipper | Carrier (driver or loading crew) |
| Purpose | Identify hazard of single package | Identify hazards present on entire vehicle |

Think of labels as the "package-level" hazard warning and placards as the "vehicle-level" hazard warning. They work together as a system -- the labels on individual packages drive the placard requirements on the vehicle carrying them.

## Section 2: The Nine Label Families

The DOT hazard classification system divides hazardous materials into nine classes, with several divisions within some classes. Each class has a distinctive label design. Learning these by sight is essential for anyone who handles hazmat packages.

### Class 1: Explosives

- **Color:** Orange background
- **Symbol:** Exploding circle (a round shape with radiating lines suggesting an explosion)
- **Divisions:** 1.1 through 1.6, indicating mass explosion hazard down to extremely insensitive articles
- **What you'll encounter:** Commercial explosives, detonators, ammunition, fireworks, flares, airbag inflators

**Fleet context:** Most fleet operators will encounter Class 1 materials rarely, if at all. When you do, be aware that Class 1 materials have the strictest segregation, loading, and routing requirements of any hazard class. Certain Class 1 divisions cannot be loaded on the same vehicle with other classes. The label division number (1.1, 1.2, etc.) matters enormously -- a 1.4S fireworks shipment has very different requirements than a 1.1D commercial explosive.

### Class 2: Gases

Gases are divided into three groups, each with a different label:

**Class 2.1: Flammable Gas**
- **Color:** Red background
- **Symbol:** Flame
- **Examples:** Propane, butane, acetylene, hydrogen, methane
- **Fleet context:** Propane cylinders, welding gas, and fuel gas are extremely common in fleet operations. The red flame label tells you this gas will burn or explode if it finds an ignition source.

**Class 2.2: Non-Flammable, Non-Toxic Gas**
- **Color:** Green background
- **Symbol:** Gas cylinder (or sometimes no symbol, just the text "NON-FLAMMABLE GAS")
- **Examples:** Compressed nitrogen, argon, helium, carbon dioxide, compressed air
- **Fleet context:** These gases do not burn, but they can displace oxygen in enclosed spaces (causing suffocation) and cylinders are under high pressure (a ruptured cylinder becomes a projectile). The green label means "not flammable," not "not dangerous."

**Class 2.3: Toxic Gas**
- **Color:** White background
- **Symbol:** Skull and crossbones
- **Examples:** Chlorine, ammonia (anhydrous), phosgene, hydrogen sulfide, carbon monoxide (in pressurized form)
- **Fleet context:** These materials can kill or cause serious injury through inhalation. Many Class 2.3 materials also require the INHALATION HAZARD marking discussed in the marking module (TNDS-HZ-004). If you see this label, the material demands the highest level of caution, maximum ventilation, and immediate evacuation if a leak occurs.

### Class 3: Flammable Liquids

- **Color:** Red background
- **Symbol:** Flame
- **Examples:** Gasoline, acetone, methanol, ethanol, toluene, paint, adhesives, many solvents
- **Fleet context:** This is the most commonly transported hazard class in fleet operations. Fuel haulers deal with Class 3 materials every day. The label looks identical to Class 2.1 (Flammable Gas) -- same red background, same flame symbol -- but the class number "3" in the bottom corner distinguishes it. Class 3 labels will be on drums, jerricans, and smaller containers of flammable liquids.

### Class 4: Flammable Solids, Spontaneous Combustion, Dangerous When Wet

Three divisions, each with a unique label design:

**Class 4.1: Flammable Solid**
- **Color:** White background with vertical red stripes
- **Symbol:** Flame
- **Examples:** Matches, sulfur, naphthalene, certain metal powders, wetted explosives
- **Fleet context:** The distinctive red-and-white striped pattern makes this label easy to spot. These materials can be ignited by friction, heat, or sparks and will continue to burn vigorously once lit.

**Class 4.2: Spontaneously Combustible**
- **Color:** Upper half white, lower half red
- **Symbol:** Flame
- **Examples:** White phosphorus, aluminum alkyls, certain oily rags and metal dusts, activated carbon
- **Fleet context:** These materials can ignite on their own without an external ignition source, simply through contact with air or through self-heating. A container of Class 4.2 material that is breached may start burning within seconds. The split white/red label is your visual cue for this unique hazard.

**Class 4.3: Dangerous When Wet**
- **Color:** Blue background
- **Symbol:** Flame
- **Examples:** Sodium, potassium, calcium carbide, certain metal powders, lithium
- **Fleet context:** These materials react violently with water, producing flammable or toxic gases. If you are hauling Class 4.3 materials and it starts raining, a breach in packaging becomes exponentially more dangerous. The blue label is your warning: keep this material bone dry. Emergency responders do NOT use water to fight fires involving Class 4.3 materials -- they use dry sand, dry chemical, or specific Class D fire extinguishers.

### Class 5: Oxidizers and Organic Peroxides

**Class 5.1: Oxidizer**
- **Color:** Yellow background
- **Symbol:** Flame over a circle (the "O" in oxidizer)
- **Examples:** Ammonium nitrate, hydrogen peroxide (concentration-dependent), sodium nitrate, calcium hypochlorite (pool shock)
- **Fleet context:** Oxidizers do not burn themselves, but they make other things burn faster and hotter by providing extra oxygen. A Class 5.1 material in contact with combustible materials (wood, paper, clothing, fuel) can cause a fire to ignite spontaneously or burn uncontrollably. Segregation from combustible materials is critical.

**Class 5.2: Organic Peroxide**
- **Color:** Upper half yellow, lower half red
- **Symbol:** Flame
- **Examples:** Benzoyl peroxide, methyl ethyl ketone peroxide, dibenzoyl peroxide
- **Fleet context:** Organic peroxides are thermally unstable and can undergo self-accelerating decomposition, generating heat, gas, and sometimes fire or explosion. Many require temperature-controlled transport. The split yellow/red label warns of both oxidizing and flammable characteristics.

### Class 6: Toxic and Infectious Substances

**Class 6.1: Toxic (Poison)**
- **Color:** White background
- **Symbol:** Skull and crossbones
- **Examples:** Pesticides, cyanide compounds, arsenic compounds, certain pharmaceutical intermediates
- **Fleet context:** These materials are harmful through ingestion, skin contact, or inhalation. The skull-and-crossbones label is universally recognized. The older term "POISON" is still seen on some labels and is acceptable. When handling packages with this label, wear appropriate PPE and avoid any skin contact with the package exterior in case of contamination.

**Class 6.2: Infectious Substance**
- **Color:** White background
- **Symbol:** Biohazard symbol (the three-interlocking-crescents design)
- **Examples:** Medical waste, diagnostic specimens, cultures, biological agents
- **Fleet context:** Most fleet operators will encounter Class 6.2 only if they serve medical facilities or laboratories. These materials contain pathogens capable of causing disease in humans or animals. Packaging is typically triple-contained (primary receptacle, secondary packaging, rigid outer packaging) with absorbent material between layers.

### Class 7: Radioactive

- **Color:** Upper half yellow (Categories II and III) or all white (Category I), lower half white
- **Symbol:** Trefoil (the three-blade radiation symbol)
- **Three categories:** Radioactive I (White), Radioactive II (Yellow), Radioactive III (Yellow) -- the category indicates the radiation level at the package surface
- **Examples:** Medical isotopes, industrial radiography sources, uranium fuel, smoke detector components (exempt quantities)
- **Fleet context:** Radioactive materials have unique transport index requirements, vehicle dose rate limits, and must be separated from people, undeveloped film, and other radioactive packages according to specific distance tables. The trefoil symbol is the universal radiation warning.

### Class 8: Corrosive

- **Color:** Upper half white, lower half black
- **Symbol:** Two images showing liquid dropping from test tubes onto a hand and a metal surface (depicting destruction of skin and metal)
- **Examples:** Sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid, sodium hydroxide (lye), battery acid, certain cleaning compounds
- **Fleet context:** Corrosives destroy living tissue and corrode metals. The dramatic label image -- acid burning through skin and metal -- is intentionally graphic. When loading or handling packages bearing this label, protect your skin, eyes, and clothing. Inspect the package closely for any signs of leakage; corrosive leaks can compromise other packages and the vehicle structure itself.

### Class 9: Miscellaneous Hazardous Materials

- **Color:** White background, upper half
- **Symbol:** Seven vertical black stripes in the upper half
- **Examples:** Lithium batteries, dry ice (solid carbon dioxide), environmentally hazardous substances, magnetized materials, elevated-temperature materials, asbestos, air bag modules
- **Fleet context:** Class 9 is the "catch-all" for materials that present a transport hazard but do not fit neatly into Classes 1 through 8. Do not let the word "miscellaneous" lead you to take these materials less seriously. Lithium batteries (the most common Class 9 material in commerce today) can undergo thermal runaway, producing intense fire and toxic fumes. Dry ice sublimates into carbon dioxide gas, which can displace oxygen in an enclosed trailer.

## Section 3: Primary and Subsidiary Labels

Many hazardous materials present more than one type of hazard. The label system handles this through **primary** and **subsidiary** (secondary) labels.

### How It Works

- The **primary label** corresponds to the material's hazard class -- the class listed in Column 3 of the Hazmat Table. This label matches the placard that will go on the vehicle.
- **Subsidiary labels** correspond to additional hazards listed in Column 6 of the Hazmat Table. These are additional diamond-shaped labels, identical in appearance to primary labels of that class, but they typically do not display the hazard class number in the bottom corner (though many shippers include it for clarity).

### Real-World Examples

**Example 1: Flammable Corrosive**

UN2924, "Flammable liquid, corrosive, n.o.s."
- Primary hazard: Class 3 (Flammable Liquid) -- red label with flame
- Subsidiary hazard: Class 8 (Corrosive) -- black/white label with acid drops

Both labels go on the package. This tells the handler: this material can catch fire AND it will destroy your skin. Wear chemical-resistant gloves and keep it away from ignition sources.

**Example 2: Toxic, Corrosive Gas**

UN1040, "Ethylene oxide" (when classified as 2.3)
- Primary hazard: Class 2.3 (Toxic Gas) -- white label with skull
- Subsidiary hazards: Class 2.1 (Flammable Gas) -- red label with flame

Both labels go on the package. This tells the responder: this gas is toxic AND flammable -- you cannot vent it safely near ignition sources.

**Example 3: Oxidizer with Poison Subsidiary**

UN2014, "Hydrogen peroxide, aqueous solution" (greater than 60%)
- Primary hazard: Class 5.1 (Oxidizer) -- yellow label with flame-over-circle
- Subsidiary hazard: Class 8 (Corrosive) -- black/white label

Both labels go on the package. High-concentration hydrogen peroxide is both a powerful oxidizer and a strong corrosive.

### Determining Subsidiary Hazards

You do not guess at subsidiary hazards. Column 6 of the Hazmat Table spells them out. If Column 6 shows "8" for a Class 3 material, that material requires a Class 3 primary label and a Class 8 subsidiary label. If Column 6 is blank, only the primary label is required.

## Section 4: Label Placement Rules (172.406)

Putting the right labels on a package is only half the job -- they must also be placed correctly. Improperly placed labels are a violation, and more importantly, they may not be seen by the people who need to see them.

### General Placement Rules

1. **Same surface as markings:** Labels must be placed on the same surface of the package where the proper shipping name and UN number are marked. The goal is that anyone reading the marking also sees the label without having to turn the package around.

2. **Near the proper shipping name:** Labels should be placed near (within readable proximity of) the proper shipping name, not on the opposite end of the package.

3. **Not on the bottom:** Labels must not be placed on the bottom of the package. The bottom may be set on the ground, on a pallet, or against other packages, rendering the label invisible.

4. **Multiple labels within 150 mm (6 inches) of each other:** When a package requires more than one label (primary plus subsidiary), all labels must be placed within 150 mm (6 inches) of each other. This keeps all hazard information together for quick assessment.

5. **Not folded over edges or seams:** Labels must be placed flat against the package surface. A label that wraps around a corner or crosses a seam cannot be read properly and may peel off.

6. **Not obscured:** Labels must not be covered by strapping, tape, stickers, other labels, or dirt. They must remain visible throughout the entire transport chain.

### Specific Situations

**Cylindrical packages (drums, cylinders):** Place labels on a flat area if available (many drums have a label panel). If the surface is curved, ensure the label adheres fully and is readable.

**Packages too small for standard labels:** For packages that cannot accommodate a 100 mm label due to their size, a label may be placed on a tag securely attached to the package. This exception applies only when the package physically cannot bear a standard-size label.

**Overpacks:** If an overpack obscures the labels on inner packages, equivalent labels must be applied to the outside of the overpack. The overpack labels must represent all the hazards present in the inner packages.

### Common Placement Mistakes

- Putting the label on the top of a box (which then gets stacked under other boxes, hiding the label)
- Placing the primary label on the front of a drum and the subsidiary label on the back (they must be within 6 inches of each other)
- Using labels that are sun-faded to the point where the color is no longer identifiable
- Placing labels over package closures where they interfere with opening or where repeated opening wears through the label

## Section 5: Exceptions from Labeling (172.400a)

Certain shipments are exempt from standard labeling requirements, though they may require alternative hazard communication marks.

### Limited Quantity Exception

Materials shipped in **limited quantities** under the provisions of 49 CFR 173.150-173.155 do not require standard hazard class labels. Instead, they require the **Limited Quantity mark** (a diamond-shaped mark with black upper and lower halves). This exception applies when:

- The material qualifies for limited quantity provisions under its specific hazard class
- Inner container quantities do not exceed the thresholds specified for that material
- The combination packaging meets the required performance standards

**Practical example:** A shipment of small cans of aerosol paint (flammable, Class 3) in retail packaging, where each can holds 340 g or less, may qualify for the limited quantity exception. Instead of a Class 3 flammable label, the outer box gets the Limited Quantity mark.

### Materials of Trade (173.6)

Certain hazmat carried as "materials of trade" -- small quantities used for the carrier's own purposes, not offered for commercial transport -- are exempt from labeling. For example, a service technician carrying a small cylinder of acetylene for field welding work. Strict quantity limits apply: no more than 1 liter (or 1 kg net mass) per container for materials in Packing Groups I or II.

### Other Exceptions

- **ORM-D** (now phased out, replaced by Limited Quantity mark): Consumer commodities previously shipped under ORM-D rules did not require hazard class labels.
- **Certain Class 9 materials**: Some Class 9 materials may have modified labeling requirements depending on their specific entry in the Hazmat Table.
- **Empty packagings**: Packages that previously contained hazmat but have been emptied and not cleaned may be shipped without labels if they meet the conditions of 49 CFR 173.29 (residue remaining is not more than what clings to the container walls, and the container is properly closed).

### What Exceptions Do NOT Mean

An exception from labeling does **not** mean the material is not hazardous. It means the quantity is small enough, or the packaging is robust enough, that reduced hazard communication is considered adequate. The material itself is still hazardous, and appropriate handling precautions still apply.

## Section 6: Matching Labels to Placards

Understanding the connection between package-level labels and vehicle-level placards is critical for fleet operations. Labels on individual packages determine what placards go on your truck.

### The Basic Rule

When you load packages bearing hazmat labels onto a vehicle, those labels dictate the placard requirements. The general rule under 49 CFR 172.504:

- **1,001 pounds or more** (aggregate gross weight) of hazmat in a single hazard class: the vehicle must be placarded for that class
- **Any quantity** of certain high-hazard materials (Table 1 materials): the vehicle must be placarded regardless of weight. Table 1 includes:
  - Class 1.1, 1.2, 1.3 (Explosives)
  - Class 2.3 (Toxic Gas)
  - Class 4.3 (Dangerous When Wet)
  - Class 6.1, PG I, Inhalation Hazard (Poison Inhalation Hazard)
  - Class 7 (Radioactive, certain quantities)

### How Labels Drive Placard Selection

| Labels on packages | Placard on vehicle |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| Class 3 (Flammable Liquid) labels, total > 1,001 lbs | FLAMMABLE placard, all four sides |
| Class 8 (Corrosive) labels, total > 1,001 lbs | CORROSIVE placard, all four sides |
| Any quantity of Class 2.3 (Toxic Gas) labels | POISON GAS placard, all four sides |
| Mixed load of Class 3 and Class 8, each > 1,001 lbs | Both FLAMMABLE and CORROSIVE placards |
| Mixed load of multiple Table 2 classes, each < 1,001 lbs but total > 1,001 lbs | DANGEROUS placard (may be used as a substitute) |

### The DANGEROUS Placard

When a vehicle carries multiple hazard classes from Table 2 (the "lower-hazard" list -- Classes 3, 4, 5, 6.1 PG III, 8, 9), and no single class exceeds 1,001 pounds but the total exceeds 1,001 pounds, the carrier may use the **DANGEROUS** placard instead of individual placards for each class. This is the placard with the word "DANGEROUS" split between a red upper half and white lower half.

**However:** The DANGEROUS placard cannot substitute for Table 1 placards. If you have any quantity of a Table 1 material, its specific placard must be displayed even if you also use the DANGEROUS placard for other classes.

### Subsidiary Labels and Placarding

Subsidiary labels on packages also affect placarding. If packages bearing a Class 8 subsidiary label total more than 1,001 pounds, the vehicle needs a CORROSIVE placard -- even though the primary hazard might be Class 3 (Flammable). Both placards go on the vehicle.

### Real-World Scenario

**Situation:** Your driver picks up a mixed load:
- 800 lbs of packages labeled Class 3 (Flammable Liquid)
- 600 lbs of packages labeled Class 8 (Corrosive)
- 200 lbs of packages labeled Class 9 (Miscellaneous)

**Total hazmat:** 1,600 lbs
**Any single class over 1,001 lbs?** No
**Total over 1,001 lbs?** Yes

**Placard option 1:** Individual placards for each class (FLAMMABLE, CORROSIVE, CLASS 9) on all four sides
**Placard option 2:** A single DANGEROUS placard on all four sides (because all are Table 2 classes and none individually exceed 1,001 lbs)

Most carriers choose the DANGEROUS placard for simplicity in mixed-load situations.

## Assessment

### Q1
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: What is the minimum size requirement for a hazmat label placed on a package?
**A**: 50 mm (2 inches) on each side
**B**: 100 mm (3.9 inches) on each side (correct)
**C**: 150 mm (5.9 inches) on each side
**D**: 273 mm (10.75 inches) on each side
**Explanation**: Hazmat labels must be diamond-shaped (square-on-point) with each side at least 100 mm (3.9 inches) in length. The 273 mm dimension is the minimum for placards, which are the larger vehicle-level hazard indicators. Labels are the smaller package-level indicators.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.407(c)

### Q2
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A package displays a label with a blue background and a flame symbol. What hazard class does this label represent?
**A**: Class 2.1 -- Flammable Gas
**B**: Class 3 -- Flammable Liquid
**C**: Class 4.3 -- Dangerous When Wet (correct)
**D**: Class 5.1 -- Oxidizer
**Explanation**: The blue background is distinctive to Class 4.3, Dangerous When Wet. While several hazard classes use the flame symbol (Classes 2.1, 3, 4.1, 4.2, 4.3), only Class 4.3 uses a blue background. This color was deliberately chosen to contrast with fire-related reds and yellows, signaling that water must NOT be used on these materials -- they react violently with water to produce flammable or toxic gases.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.411, 172.415

### Q3
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A hazardous material is classified as Class 3 (Flammable Liquid) with a subsidiary hazard of Class 8 (Corrosive) listed in Column 6 of the Hazmat Table. How many labels must be placed on each package?
**A**: One -- only the primary Class 3 label is required
**B**: Two -- a Class 3 primary label and a Class 8 subsidiary label (correct)
**C**: Two -- a Class 3 label and a DANGEROUS label
**D**: Three -- a Class 3 label, a Class 8 label, and a Class 9 label
**Explanation**: When a material has a subsidiary hazard listed in Column 6 of the Hazmat Table, a subsidiary label must be applied in addition to the primary label. The primary Class 3 (Flammable Liquid) label indicates the dominant hazard, and the subsidiary Class 8 (Corrosive) label communicates the additional corrosive hazard. Both labels must be placed within 150 mm (6 inches) of each other on the same surface.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.402

### Q4
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: Where must hazmat labels be placed on a package?
**A**: On the top of the package for easy visibility during stacking
**B**: On the same surface as the proper shipping name and UN number marking, not on the bottom of the package (correct)
**C**: On the bottom of the package to protect the label from weather damage
**D**: On any surface, as long as the label is visible when the package is in storage
**Explanation**: Labels must be placed on the same surface where the proper shipping name and identification number are marked, so all hazard information is visible together. Labels must not be placed on the bottom of the package because the bottom may rest against the floor, pallet, or other packages, making the label invisible. Labels must remain unobscured throughout transport.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.406(a)

**D**: Red background with vertical white stripes and a flame symbol

### Q6
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A vehicle is loaded with 1,500 pounds of packages bearing Class 3 (Flammable Liquid) labels. No other hazmat is on board. What placard is required on the vehicle?
**A**: DANGEROUS placard on two sides
**B**: FLAMMABLE placard on all four sides (correct)
**C**: CLASS 3 placard on the rear only
**D**: No placard is required because the load is under 2,000 pounds
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 172.504, when the aggregate gross weight of hazardous materials in a single hazard class (Table 2) exceeds 1,001 pounds, the vehicle must display the appropriate placard on all four sides (front, rear, and both sides). With 1,500 pounds of Class 3 materials, the FLAMMABLE placard is required on all four sides of the vehicle.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.504

### Q7
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: Which of the following materials requires placarding at ANY quantity, regardless of weight, because it appears on the Table 1 list of high-hazard materials?
**A**: Class 3, Packing Group III (Flammable Liquid, minor hazard)
**B**: Class 8, Packing Group II (Corrosive, medium hazard)
**C**: Class 2.3 (Toxic Gas) (correct)
**D**: Class 9 (Miscellaneous Hazardous Material)
**Explanation**: Table 1 of 49 CFR 172.504 lists the highest-hazard categories that require placarding at any quantity: Class 1.1/1.2/1.3 (Explosives), Class 2.3 (Toxic Gas), Class 4.3 (Dangerous When Wet), Class 6.1 PG I Inhalation Hazard, and Class 7 (Radioactive, certain quantities). All other hazard classes fall under Table 2 and require placarding only when the total exceeds 1,001 pounds. Toxic gases are so dangerous that even a single package on a vehicle triggers the placard requirement.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.504(e), Table 1
