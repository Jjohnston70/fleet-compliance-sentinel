# Carrier Requirements: Vessel

## Learning Objectives
- Describe the scope of hazmat transported by vessel and the role of the IMDG Code
- Explain stowage requirements including "on deck" vs "under deck" categories
- Apply vessel segregation rules for incompatible materials
- Identify documentation requirements for domestic and international marine hazmat shipments
- Recognize marine pollutant marking and reporting requirements
- Describe the U.S. Coast Guard's role in port and waterway hazmat safety
- Explain how vessel hazmat rules affect highway fleet operators who serve port facilities

## Section 1: Vessel Transport Overview

Marine vessels move the largest single quantities of hazardous materials of any transportation mode in the world. A single chemical tanker can carry 10,000 to 50,000 metric tons of product. A large container ship may carry 20,000 containers, hundreds of which may contain hazardous materials. Inland barges on the Mississippi River system routinely carry 10,000 to 30,000 barrels of petroleum products per barge, with barge tows consisting of 15 or more barges lashed together.

The materials that move by vessel include crude oil, refined petroleum products, liquefied natural gas (LNG), industrial chemicals, agricultural chemicals, explosives, radioactive materials, and every other hazard class. International maritime trade is the backbone of the global chemical supply chain.

### The Regulatory Framework: Two Sets of Rules

Vessel transport of hazmat operates under two overlapping regulatory systems:

**U.S. Domestic Rules (49 CFR Part 176)**: PHMSA regulations that apply to domestic waterway movements within the United States. These rules govern stowage, segregation, and handling of hazmat on vessels operating in U.S. waters. For domestic movements, the standard U.S. hazmat shipping papers (bills of lading that comply with 49 CFR Part 172) are generally sufficient.

**International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) Code**: Published by the International Maritime Organization (IMO), the IMDG Code is the international standard for transporting hazardous materials by sea. It applies to all international marine shipments. The IMDG Code and the U.S. HMR are broadly aligned, but they are not identical. Where the shipment crosses international waters or involves a foreign port, the IMDG Code requirements apply in addition to U.S. domestic rules. The IMDG Code uses the same UN number and proper shipping name system as the HMR, so the classification framework is familiar, but specific packaging, stowage, and documentation details may differ.

For fleet operators, the practical takeaway is this: if the cargo is moving domestically by barge or coastwise vessel, U.S. rules under Part 176 govern. If the cargo is heading overseas on a container ship, the shipper must also comply with the IMDG Code. Your job as the highway carrier is to deliver cargo that is properly classified, packaged, and documented so it meets the requirements for the vessel leg.

### Historical Context

Major maritime hazmat incidents have shaped current regulations:

**SS Grandcamp, Texas City (1947)**: A cargo ship loaded with ammonium nitrate fertilizer caught fire and exploded in the port of Texas City, Texas, killing nearly 600 people and destroying a significant portion of the city. This remains one of the deadliest industrial disasters in U.S. history and led to fundamental changes in how hazmat is stowed and handled in port.

**Exxon Valdez, Alaska (1989)**: A tanker ran aground in Prince William Sound, releasing 11 million gallons of crude oil. While primarily an environmental disaster rather than a hazmat transportation violation, this incident led to the Oil Pollution Act of 1990 and strengthened vessel safety and response planning requirements.

**MSC Flaminia, Atlantic Ocean (2012)**: A container ship suffered a fire and explosion caused by improperly declared hazardous materials (divinylbenzene) in the cargo hold. Three crew members were killed. The vessel drifted in the Atlantic for weeks before a port would accept it. This case highlighted the danger of undeclared or misdeclared hazmat in container shipping.

## Section 2: Stowage Requirements (176.76-84)

Stowage is the maritime term for where and how cargo is physically placed on a vessel. For hazardous materials, stowage is not optional or left to the judgment of the dock crew — it is prescribed by regulation based on the hazard characteristics of the material.

### On Deck vs Under Deck

The two fundamental stowage locations on a vessel are:

**On Deck**: The cargo is placed on the weather deck (the top of the ship, exposed to the elements). On-deck stowage provides natural ventilation — vapors dissipate into the open air rather than accumulating in an enclosed hold. Crew can access on-deck cargo more easily for inspection and emergency response. However, on-deck cargo is exposed to weather, wave action, and temperature extremes.

**Under Deck**: The cargo is placed in an enclosed cargo hold below the weather deck. Under-deck stowage protects cargo from weather and is used for materials that must be kept dry or at controlled temperatures. However, under-deck cargo is in an enclosed space where leaking vapors can accumulate to dangerous concentrations, and access for emergency response is more limited.

### Stowage Categories

The Hazardous Materials Table in 49 CFR 172.101 includes stowage information in **Column 10A**, which specifies the stowage category for each material. The IMDG Code uses a parallel system. The stowage categories include:

- **Category A**: May be stowed on deck or under deck. The material does not pose a significant ventilation concern.
- **Category B**: Must be stowed on deck only, or in a mechanically ventilated space under deck. This applies to materials whose vapors need to dissipate — such as certain flammable liquids and toxic gases.
- **Category C**: Must be stowed on deck only. No under-deck stowage is permitted, even with mechanical ventilation. This applies to materials like organic peroxides and certain oxidizers that need both ventilation and accessibility.
- **Category D**: Must be stowed on deck only and "protected from sources of heat." These materials are both vapor-hazardous and heat-sensitive.
- **Category E**: Must be stowed on deck or under deck, but "away from sources of heat." The material can go below deck but must not be near engine rooms, heated fuel tanks, or other heat sources.

### Why Stowage Matters for Highway Carriers

When you deliver a container of hazmat to a port, the stowage category determines where the shipping line places that container on the vessel. If the cargo is Category C (on deck only), the container must be placed on the top tiers of the deck stack. The documentation you deliver with the cargo must accurately describe the material so the vessel operator can assign the correct stowage. Incorrect or incomplete documentation can cause the container to be stowed in the wrong location — a serious safety violation that puts the vessel, crew, and cargo at risk.

**Real-World Scenario**: Your fleet delivers a 20-foot container of organic peroxides (Division 5.2) to a container terminal. Organic peroxides are stowage Category D — on deck only, protected from sources of heat. The shipping documentation your driver provides at the gate must clearly identify the material with the correct proper shipping name, UN number, and hazard class so the terminal operator assigns the container to an on-deck slot away from the engine room stack and any containers generating heat. If the paperwork says "industrial chemicals" without specifying the hazard, the container might be stowed under deck — creating a serious fire and explosion risk.

## Section 3: Segregation on Vessels (176.83)

Segregation on a vessel follows the same general principle as highway segregation — incompatible materials must be separated so that a leak or fire in one cannot trigger a reaction in another. But the vessel segregation system is more detailed than the highway system because of the physical structure of ships.

### The Four Levels of Vessel Segregation

The IMDG Code and 49 CFR 176.83 define four levels of segregation, from least to most restrictive:

**1. "Away from"**: The materials must be placed in different cargo spaces or holds, or at least 3 meters (10 feet) apart if in the same cargo space. This is the lowest level of separation — the materials should not be immediately adjacent but do not require structural barriers between them.

**2. "Separated from"**: The materials must be in different cargo compartments or holds. If on deck, they must be separated by a distance of at least 6 meters (20 feet) horizontally. A single bulkhead (wall between compartments) satisfies this requirement for under-deck stowage. This means the materials must have a real physical barrier or meaningful distance between them.

**3. "Separated by a complete compartment from"**: The materials must have at least one complete cargo compartment between them. If material A is in Hold 1 and material B is in Hold 3, with Hold 2 (containing neither A nor B) between them, this requirement is met. On deck, this translates to a separation of at least 12 meters (40 feet) horizontally. One shared bulkhead is not enough — there must be an intervening space.

**4. "Separated longitudinally by an intervening complete compartment from"**: This is the most restrictive level. The materials must be separated along the length of the ship by an entire intermediate cargo compartment. This level applies to the most dangerous combinations — materials that, if they came into contact, could produce catastrophic reactions such as explosion, toxic gas generation, or uncontrolled fire.

### How Segregation Is Determined

The IMDG Code includes a segregation table that cross-references hazard classes. For example:

- Class 1 (Explosives) must be "separated longitudinally by an intervening complete compartment from" Division 2.1 (Flammable Gas).
- Class 3 (Flammable Liquid) must be "away from" Class 5.1 (Oxidizer).
- Division 4.3 (Dangerous When Wet) must be "separated from" Class 8 (Corrosive).

The specific materials within each class may have additional segregation requirements listed in the IMDG Code supplement or in the individual material entries.

### Practical Impact

For a large container ship carrying thousands of containers, the segregation requirements are a three-dimensional puzzle. The vessel's cargo planner must account for stowage categories (on deck vs under deck), segregation distances (which classes cannot be in the same hold), and physical ship constraints (hold sizes, ventilation capability, access routes). This is why accurate hazmat declarations are absolutely critical — the planner cannot solve the puzzle if they do not know what is in the containers.

**Real-World Scenario**: A container ship is loading at a U.S. port. Hold 2 already has containers of Class 5.1 oxidizers. A truck arrives at the terminal gate with a container of Division 4.1 flammable solids. The segregation table requires these be "separated from" — they must be in different holds or at least 6 meters apart on deck. The terminal operator must place the Division 4.1 container in a different hold or an on-deck position sufficiently distant from the Hold 2 stack. If the container's documentation does not accurately reflect its contents, the operator might place it in Hold 2 next to the oxidizers — a combination that could produce an intense, self-sustaining fire.

## Section 4: Documentation Requirements

Marine hazmat shipments require specific documentation that goes beyond the standard highway bill of lading.

### Dangerous Goods Declaration (Marine)

The Dangerous Goods Declaration is the marine equivalent of the Shipper's Declaration used in air transport. For international shipments governed by the IMDG Code, this document must include:

- The shipper's name and address
- The consignee's name and address
- The proper shipping name, UN number, hazard class, packing group, and quantity
- The number and type of packages
- The flash point (for flammable liquids with a flash point of 60 degrees C / 140 degrees F or below)
- Marine pollutant designation (if applicable)
- EmS (Emergency Schedule) number — the IMDG equivalent of the ERG guide number
- A signed certification that the cargo is properly classified, packaged, marked, labeled, and in proper condition for carriage by sea

For domestic waterway shipments within the United States, a standard shipping paper complying with 49 CFR 172.200-204 is generally acceptable. However, if the cargo will be loaded onto a vessel that also carries international cargo, the vessel operator may require IMDG-format documentation for all hazmat on board to simplify their compliance tracking.

### Container/Vehicle Packing Certificate

When hazmat is loaded into a freight container or vehicle for transport by vessel, the packer must sign a **Container/Vehicle Packing Certificate**. This certificate states that:

- The container or vehicle was clean, dry, and fit for the cargo
- Incompatible materials were not packed together unless permitted by regulation
- All packages were inspected for damage and leaks before loading
- Packages were properly secured inside the container to prevent shifting
- The container is properly marked and placarded on the outside

This certificate may be combined with the Dangerous Goods Declaration or provided as a separate document. The vessel operator requires this certificate before accepting the container for loading.

### Bulk Liquid Cargo Documentation

For bulk liquid hazmat carried in tanker vessels or barges, additional documentation includes the cargo manifest (listing all tanks and their contents), compatibility certificates (confirming the material is compatible with the tank coatings and previous cargoes), and certificates of fitness for the vessel itself.

## Section 5: Marine Pollutants

Materials identified as **marine pollutants** in Appendix B to the Hazardous Materials Table (49 CFR 172.101) are subject to additional requirements when transported by vessel. A marine pollutant is a material that poses a significant risk to the marine environment if released into waterways.

### Identification

The Hazardous Materials Table identifies marine pollutants with a "G" designation in Column 1. You can also find the complete list in Appendix B to Section 172.101. Examples of common marine pollutants include certain pesticides, heavy metal compounds, petroleum distillates, and chlorinated solvents.

### Marking Requirements

Packages containing marine pollutants must be marked with the **marine pollutant mark** — a distinctive symbol showing a fish and tree inside a diamond (or triangle for single packagings). This mark must be in addition to any other marks and labels required for the material's hazard class. The mark alerts vessel crews and port workers that a spill of this material into the water requires immediate notification and response.

For bulk shipments, the marine pollutant mark must be displayed on the tank or container in addition to the standard placards.

### Reporting Requirements

If a marine pollutant is released into navigable waters of the United States, the responsible party must notify the **National Response Center (NRC)** at 1-800-424-8802. This reporting requirement exists under both DOT regulations and the Clean Water Act. The Coast Guard coordinates the response.

For vessel operators, any discharge of a marine pollutant — even a small amount from a leaking container on deck — triggers a reporting obligation. The threshold is lower than for most hazmat spill reporting because of the direct environmental impact on marine ecosystems.

**Real-World Scenario**: Your fleet delivers a flatbed load of drums containing a chlorinated solvent (Class 6.1, Toxic, also designated as a marine pollutant) to a container loading facility at a port. The shipper has applied the Class 6.1 TOXIC labels on each drum, but the marine pollutant mark is missing. The loading facility spots this during their pre-loading inspection and refuses to accept the drums until the marks are applied. Your driver is stuck at the gate while the shipper sends someone out with the correct marks. This delay could have been prevented if the shipper had applied both the TOXIC labels and the marine pollutant marks at the origin — or if your driver had checked the drums against the shipping papers before departing.

## Section 6: Port and Waterway Safety

The **United States Coast Guard (USCG)** is the primary federal agency responsible for enforcing hazmat safety on vessels and at port facilities. While PHMSA writes the hazmat rules in 49 CFR Part 176, the Coast Guard enforces them on the water and at the dock.

### Captain of the Port (COTP)

Every major port area in the United States has a designated **Captain of the Port (COTP)** — a Coast Guard officer who has broad authority over vessel safety, port security, and hazmat operations within the port zone. The COTP can:

- Require vessels to stop, anchor, or divert if hazmat is improperly stowed or documented
- Restrict vessel movements during hazmat loading or unloading operations
- Order evacuations of port areas if a hazmat release occurs
- Impose penalties for violations of hazmat and port safety regulations
- Close a port to traffic during a hazmat emergency

### Vessel Response Plans

Vessels carrying oil or certain hazardous materials must maintain a **Vessel Response Plan (VRP)** that describes how the vessel operator will respond to a spill or release. The VRP must identify:

- Response resources (cleanup contractors, spill response equipment, trained personnel) that are pre-arranged and on call
- Notification procedures (Coast Guard, National Response Center, state agencies)
- Worst-case discharge scenarios and the planned response to each
- Communication protocols between the vessel, the response contractor, and government agencies

These plans must be approved by the Coast Guard and carried on board the vessel.

### Notice of Arrival

Vessels carrying hazmat must provide a **Notice of Arrival (NOA)** to the Coast Guard before entering a U.S. port. The NOA must include information about the vessel, crew, cargo (including all hazmat), and last port of call. For vessels arriving from foreign ports, the NOA must be submitted at least 96 hours before arrival. For domestic voyages, the timeline is shorter (typically 24 hours). The NOA allows the Coast Guard to assess risks, assign boarding priorities, and pre-position response resources if needed.

### Port Facility Security

Port facilities that handle hazmat are subject to the Maritime Transportation Security Act (MTSA) and must have approved facility security plans. These plans address access control, surveillance, cargo handling procedures, and response to security threats. Fleet drivers delivering hazmat to port facilities will encounter security checkpoints, identification requirements, and restricted access areas that are part of this regulatory framework.

**Real-World Scenario**: Your driver arrives at a container terminal to deliver a load of Class 3 flammable liquids destined for vessel transport. At the terminal gate, the driver must present a valid Transportation Worker Identification Credential (TWIC) card, the shipping papers for the hazmat cargo, and the container/vehicle packing certificate. The terminal security officer verifies the TWIC, inspects the documentation, and checks the container placards before granting entry. Without a TWIC, the driver cannot enter the secure area of the port — there is no workaround and no escort option at many facilities. If your fleet delivers to port facilities, every driver who enters the marine terminal must have a current TWIC card.

## Section 7: Relevance to Fleet Operators

Even if your fleet never loads a barge or sails on a ship, vessel hazmat rules matter to you if any of these situations apply:

### Delivering to Port Terminals

If your trucks deliver hazmat containers or cargo to a marine container terminal or bulk facility, you are the last land-based link before the cargo goes to sea. The documentation your driver delivers with the cargo must be accurate and complete. Errors in the proper shipping name, hazard class, or marine pollutant designation can cause:

- The container to be rejected at the terminal gate
- Incorrect stowage on the vessel (wrong hold, wrong deck position)
- Segregation violations (incompatible materials placed too close together)
- Regulatory violations that trace back to the shipper but affect the entire supply chain

### Picking Up from Port Terminals

When your fleet picks up a container from a marine terminal, the container may have been packed overseas and shipped by vessel. The markings, labels, and documentation should comply with both IMDG Code and U.S. HMR requirements. Your driver should verify:

- Placards are present on all four sides plus the top (if required)
- The shipping papers are present and legible
- The container shows no signs of leakage, damage, or shifting cargo (listen for sloshing, look for stains or residue around doors)
- Marine pollutant marks are present if required

### Understanding Stowage Categories for Documentation Accuracy

The stowage category for a material is determined by its hazard class and specific properties. As a highway carrier, you do not assign the stowage category — the vessel operator does that. But the vessel operator relies on the shipping documents you deliver. If the documents are wrong, the stowage assignment will be wrong. Getting the proper shipping name, hazard class, and packing group right on the highway shipping paper ensures the cargo is stowed correctly once it reaches the vessel.

### TWIC Card Requirements

Drivers who enter secure areas of marine port facilities must hold a valid **Transportation Worker Identification Credential (TWIC)** issued by TSA. The TWIC card requires a background check and takes several weeks to process. If your fleet regularly delivers to or picks up from ports, every driver assigned to port runs must have a current TWIC. An expired TWIC or a driver without one means the load cannot be delivered — the terminal will turn the truck away at the gate.

## Key Takeaways
- Vessels carry the largest quantities of hazmat of any mode, and the IMDG Code supplements U.S. regulations for international shipments
- Stowage rules (on deck vs under deck) are determined by the material's stowage category and directly affect safety
- Vessel segregation has four levels, from "away from" (3 meters) to "separated longitudinally by an intervening complete compartment from" (most restrictive)
- Marine pollutants require a specific mark (fish and tree symbol) and trigger reporting if released into waterways
- The Coast Guard enforces vessel hazmat rules through the Captain of the Port, vessel response plans, and notice of arrival requirements
- Highway fleet operators need accurate documentation and TWIC cards when delivering to or picking up from port facilities

**Question**: What regulatory code supplements U.S. domestic rules (49 CFR Part 176) for international shipments of hazardous materials by vessel?
**A**: IATA Dangerous Goods Regulations
**B**: International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) Code
**C**: ICAO Technical Instructions
**D**: NFPA 472 Hazmat Operations Standard
**Correct Answer**: B
**Explanation**: The International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) Code, published by the International Maritime Organization (IMO), is the international standard for transporting hazardous materials by sea. It supplements 49 CFR Part 176 for any shipment moving through international waters or involving a foreign port. IATA rules apply to air transport, and ICAO Technical Instructions also apply to air transport.

### Q5
**Type**: scenario
**Question**: Your driver arrives at a marine container terminal to deliver a container of hazmat for vessel transport. The terminal security officer asks for a TWIC card, shipping papers, and a container packing certificate. Your driver has the shipping papers but does not have a TWIC card. What happens?
**A**: The driver can enter the terminal if escorted by terminal security staff
**B**: The driver can present a CDL with hazmat endorsement as an alternative to the TWIC card
**C**: The driver will be denied entry to the secure area of the terminal — the delivery cannot be completed without a valid TWIC card
**D**: The driver can enter the terminal but only during daylight hours
**Correct Answer**: C
**Explanation**: Marine terminal secure areas require a Transportation Worker Identification Credential (TWIC) issued by TSA. A CDL with hazmat endorsement is not a substitute.
