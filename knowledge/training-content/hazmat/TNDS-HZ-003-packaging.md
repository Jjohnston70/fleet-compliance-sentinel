# TNDS-HZ-003: Packaging Requirements

## Learning Objectives

After completing this module, you will be able to:

- Explain why proper hazmat packaging is the first line of defense against incidents
- Distinguish between Performance-Oriented Packaging Standards and older specification packaging
- Identify non-bulk and bulk packaging types by their UN codes
- Read and interpret UN certification markings on packaging
- Use the Hazmat Table (Columns 8, 9A, 9B) to determine authorized packagings
- Recognize common packaging violations that lead to enforcement actions

---

## Section 1: Why Packaging Matters

Packaging is the **first and most important barrier** between a hazardous material and the people, property, and environment around it. Every other safety measure -- markings, labels, placards, shipping papers, emergency response -- assumes that the packaging is doing its job. When packaging fails, everything else becomes damage control.

### What Happens When Packaging Fails

Consider these real-world scenarios:

**Scenario A: The Leaking Drum.** A fleet driver picks up a load of 55-gallon drums containing a Class 8 corrosive liquid (hydrochloric acid). One drum has a hairline crack near the bottom chime that nobody caught during loading. Thirty miles down the highway, the acid eats through the crack, pools on the trailer floor, and begins corroding the trailer decking. By the time the driver notices fumes in the cab and pulls over, three drums have been compromised by the spreading acid. The highway is shut down for six hours. Cleanup cost: $340,000. PHMSA civil penalty: $81,000.

**Scenario B: The Wrong Box.** A shipping clerk packs a small quantity of flammable adhesive into a regular corrugated cardboard box -- not a UN-certified 4G fiberboard box. The package gets stacked under heavy freight during transit. The box crushes, the inner containers break, and flammable vapors fill the trailer. A spark from a forklift ignites the vapors during unloading. Two workers are hospitalized with burns.

**Scenario C: The Overfilled Jerrycan.** A 20-liter plastic jerrycan is filled to the very top with a Class 3 flammable liquid. On a hot summer day, thermal expansion pushes the liquid past the closure seal. The leaking liquid contacts the hot exhaust system of the transport vehicle.

These are not hypothetical. PHMSA incident reports document hundreds of packaging-related failures every year. The common thread: someone cut a corner on packaging, and the hazmat did what hazmat does.

### The Regulatory Foundation

Under 49 CFR Part 173, every person who offers a hazardous material for transportation is responsible for ensuring the packaging is:

- **Authorized** for that specific material (not just "any container that fits")
- **Properly constructed** to UN or DOT specifications
- **In good condition** with no damage, corrosion, or defects
- **Correctly closed** according to the manufacturer's closure instructions
- **Not overfilled** beyond the maximum capacity or fill level

The shipper bears primary responsibility, but carriers have a duty too: you cannot transport a package you know or should know is in violation.

## Section 2: Performance-Oriented Packaging Standards (POP)

### The Shift from "How to Build It" to "What It Must Survive"

Before 1990, DOT told manufacturers exactly how to build hazmat packaging -- what gauge of steel, what thickness of fiberboard, what type of welds. This was called **specification packaging**. The problem: it stifled innovation and did not always guarantee that the package would actually protect its contents.

Starting in 1991, the U.S. adopted the **Performance-Oriented Packaging Standards (POP)** system, aligned with the United Nations Recommendations on the Transport of Dangerous Goods. Instead of dictating construction methods, POP says: *"Build it however you want, but it must pass these tests."*

### The Four Core Tests

Every UN-certified package must pass a series of tests that simulate the worst-case handling conditions it will face during transport. The rigor of testing depends on the **Packing Group** of the material it will hold.

#### 1. Drop Test

The filled package is dropped from a specific height onto a rigid, flat surface:

| Packing Group | Drop Height |
|---------------|-------------|
| PG I (Great Danger) | 1.8 meters (5.9 feet) |
| PG II (Medium Danger) | 1.2 meters (3.9 feet) |
| PG III (Minor Danger) | 0.8 meters (2.6 feet) |

The package must survive the drop with no leakage of contents. Think about it: PG I materials are so dangerous that their packaging must survive being dropped from nearly 6 feet onto concrete -- roughly the height of a tall worker's hands.

#### 2. Stacking Test

The package must withstand a load equivalent to being stacked in a transport vehicle or warehouse for 24 hours. The test load simulates the weight of identical packages stacked 3 meters (10 feet) high. This ensures a bottom-layer drum does not get crushed by the weight above it.

#### 3. Leakproofness Test (Liquids Only)

Packages intended for liquids are pressurized with air to verify they do not leak. The test pressure varies by Packing Group:

| Packing Group | Minimum Air Pressure |
|---------------|----------------------|
| PG I | 30 kPa (4.4 psi) |
| PG II | 20 kPa (2.9 psi) |
| PG III | 20 kPa (2.9 psi) |

No air bubbles may appear. This catches hairline cracks, faulty gaskets, and poorly seated closures.

#### 4. Hydraulic Pressure Test (Drums and Jerricans for Liquids)

Metal and plastic drums and jerricans must withstand internal hydraulic (water) pressure without leaking or deforming permanently. The test pressure depends on the vapor pressure of the intended contents and the Packing Group.

### What Packing Groups Really Mean

Packing Groups are assigned based on how dangerous the material is within its hazard class:

- **Packing Group I** -- Great Danger. The most hazardous materials in the class. Example: a highly toxic liquid with an oral LD50 below 5 mg/kg. Packaging must pass the most rigorous tests.
- **Packing Group II** -- Medium Danger. Moderately hazardous. Example: gasoline (Class 3 flammable liquid). Packaging is tested to intermediate standards.
- **Packing Group III** -- Minor Danger. Lower hazard within the class. Example: diesel fuel (Class 3 flammable liquid). Packaging is tested to the base standard.

**Key point for fleet operators:** If a package is certified for PG I, it can also be used for PG II and PG III materials in the same hazard class. But a PG III package **cannot** be used for PG I materials. Higher certification always covers lower packing groups, not the other way around.

Not all hazard classes use Packing Groups. Classes 1 (Explosives), 2 (Gases), 6.2 (Infectious Substances), and 7 (Radioactive) have their own specialized packaging requirements.

## Section 3: Types of Packaging

### Non-Bulk Packaging

Non-bulk packaging has a maximum capacity of:
- **450 liters (119 gallons)** for liquids
- **400 kg (882 pounds)** net mass for solids
- **454 kg (1,000 pounds)** water capacity for gases

These are the packages your drivers handle individually -- drums, boxes, cans, bags, and cylinders.

#### Common Non-Bulk Packaging Types

| UN Code | Description | Typical Use |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| 1A1 | Steel drum, closed head (non-removable top) | Liquid chemicals, solvents |
| 1A2 | Steel drum, open head (removable top) | Solid chemicals, pastes |
| 1H1 | Plastic drum, closed head | Corrosive liquids (acid resistant) |
| 1H2 | Plastic drum, open head | Solid wastes, powders |
| 3A1 | Steel jerrycan, closed head | Small-volume flammable liquids |
| 3H1 | Plastic jerrycan, closed head | Small-volume corrosive liquids |
| 4G | Fiberboard box | Consumer products, small inner containers |
| 4C1 | Natural wood box, ordinary | Heavier items requiring rigid packaging |
| 4D | Plywood box | Medium-weight items |
| 5H3 | Woven plastic bag | Dry solid materials |
| 5M2 | Multi-wall paper bag | Powders, granules |
| 6HA1 | Composite packaging (plastic inner, steel outer) | Highly aggressive liquids |

**How to Read the UN Packaging Code:**

The first number indicates the packaging type:
- **1** = Drum
- 2 = (Reserved)
- **3** = Jerrycan
- **4** = Box
- **5** = Bag
- **6** = Composite packaging

The letter indicates the material:
- **A** = Steel
- **B** = Aluminum
- **C** = Natural wood
- **D** = Plywood
- **G** = Fiberboard
- **H** = Plastic
- **L** = Textile
- **M** = Paper, multi-wall

The final number (for drums and jerricans):
- **1** = Closed head (non-removable top, has bungs)
- **2** = Open head (removable top with ring closure)

So when you see **1A2** on a drum, you instantly know: drum (1), steel (A), open head (2).

#### Cylinders

Compressed gas cylinders follow a different system under 49 CFR Part 178, Subpart C. Common DOT specifications include:

- **DOT 3AA** -- Seamless steel cylinder (common for oxygen, nitrogen, argon)
- **DOT 4BA** -- Welded steel cylinder (lower pressure applications)
- **DOT 39** -- Non-reusable steel cylinder (refrigerants, small quantities)
- **DOT 3AL** -- Seamless aluminum cylinder (medical oxygen, scuba)

Cylinders must be retested at intervals specified on the cylinder (typically every 5 or 10 years). A cylinder past its retest date **cannot** be offered for transportation while filled.

### Bulk Packaging

Bulk packaging exceeds the non-bulk thresholds. For fleet operators, the most important bulk packagings are cargo tanks and portable tanks.

#### Cargo Tank Motor Vehicles

These are the tanker trucks you see on the highway. The old "MC" (Motor Carrier) designations are being replaced by "DOT" specifications, but both are still in service:

| Old Spec | New Spec | Typical Contents |
|----------|----------|------------------|
| MC 306 | DOT 406 | Gasoline, diesel, fuel oil (flammable/combustible liquids) |
| MC 307 | DOT 407 | Mild corrosives, low-pressure chemicals |
| MC 312 | DOT 412 | Strong corrosives (sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid) |
| MC 331 | (no DOT equiv) | Liquefied compressed gases (propane, anhydrous ammonia) |
| MC 338 | (no DOT equiv) | Cryogenic liquids (liquid oxygen, liquid nitrogen) |

**Real-world tip:** If your fleet operates DOT 406 cargo tanks (the most common fuel haulers), know that these tanks are **non-pressure** vessels designed for atmospheric-pressure liquids. They have a characteristic oval cross-section and aluminum construction. They are **not** rated for compressed gases or high-vapor-pressure liquids.

#### Intermediate Bulk Containers (IBCs)

IBCs bridge the gap between drums and cargo tanks. They hold between 450 liters and 3,000 liters (roughly 119 to 793 gallons). Common types:

- **31A** -- Metal IBC for liquids
- **31H1** -- Rigid plastic IBC for liquids (the white-caged totes you see everywhere)
- **11H1** -- Rigid plastic IBC for solids
- **13H1** -- Flexible plastic IBC (liner in a rigid frame)

IBCs have their own UN markings and retest requirements. Rigid plastic IBCs used for liquids have a **maximum service life** and must be inspected before reuse.

#### Portable Tanks

UN portable tanks (also called ISO tanks or intermodal tanks) are designed for multimodal transport -- they can go on a truck chassis, a rail car, or a container ship. They follow the T-code system (T1 through T22), with higher numbers indicating more robust construction for more dangerous materials.

## Section 4: UN Markings on Packaging

Every UN-certified package carries a standardized marking that tells you exactly what that package is rated for. Learning to read these markings is one of the most practical skills a fleet operator can develop.

### The UN Certification Mark Format

A complete UN marking looks like this:

```
     u
     n       4G / Y25 / S / 02 / USA / ACME
     ⬡
  (UN symbol)
```

Let's break down each element:

| Position | Example | Meaning |
|----------|---------|---------|
| UN symbol | u/n in a circle or ⬡ | Certifies this is a UN-tested package |
| Packaging code | 4G | Fiberboard box |
| Performance level | Y | Tested to Packing Group II standards |
| Maximum gross mass or density | 25 | 25 kg maximum gross mass (for solids) |
| State of contents | S | Tested with solids (S = solid, L = liquid) |
| Year of manufacture | 02 | Manufactured in 2002 |
| Country of authorization | USA | Testing authorized in the United States |
| Manufacturer | ACME | Name or code of the manufacturer |

### Performance Levels: X, Y, Z

This single letter is critical:

- **X** -- Meets PG I, II, and III standards (the toughest). Can be used for any packing group.
- **Y** -- Meets PG II and III standards. **Cannot** be used for PG I materials.
- **Z** -- Meets PG III standards only. Can only be used for the least hazardous materials.

**Fleet operator tip:** When you receive a package for transport, check that the performance level on the UN marking is sufficient for the Packing Group of the material inside. If the shipping papers say the material is PG I but the drum is marked "Y," that package is not authorized -- do not transport it.

### Reading UN Markings in Practice

**Example 1:** `UN 1A1/X1.8/300 / 06 / USA / GREIF`
- 1A1 = Steel drum, closed head
- X = Rated for PG I, II, or III
- 1.8 = Tested at 1.8 specific gravity (tells you the maximum density of liquid it can hold)
- 300 = Hydraulic test pressure in kPa
- 06 = Manufactured in 2006
- USA = Authorized in the United States
- GREIF = Manufacturer (Greif Inc.)

**Example 2:** `UN 4G/Y25/S / 23 / USA / IP`
- 4G = Fiberboard box
- Y = Rated for PG II and III only
- 25 = Maximum gross mass 25 kg
- S = Tested with solids
- 23 = Manufactured in 2023
- USA = Authorized in the United States
- IP = Manufacturer (International Paper)

**Example 3:** `UN 31H1/Y/1600/250 / 24 / USA / SCHTZ`
- 31H1 = Rigid plastic IBC for liquids
- Y = Rated for PG II and III
- 1600 = Maximum stacking test load in kg
- 250 = Maximum permitted filling pressure in kPa
- 24 = Manufactured in 2024
- USA = Authorized in the United States
- SCHTZ = Manufacturer (Schuetz)

### When Markings Are Missing or Illegible

If you cannot read the UN marking on a package, treat the packaging as **unauthorized**. It may be an old specification package, a non-UN domestic container, or a damaged package whose markings have worn off. In any of these cases, the package should not be offered for transportation until the packaging is verified or the material is repackaged into properly marked containers.

## Section 5: Authorized Packagings by Hazard Class

### Using the Hazmat Table (49 CFR 172.101)

The Hazmat Table is the master reference for everything about transporting a specific hazardous material. For packaging, you need **Column 8** -- the three sub-columns labeled 8A, 8B, and 8C:

- **Column 8A** -- Packaging exceptions (sections in Part 173 that allow simplified or reduced packaging)
- **Column 8B** -- Non-bulk packaging (the specific section in Part 173 authorizing non-bulk packages)
- **Column 8C** -- Bulk packaging (the specific section in Part 173 authorizing bulk packages)

#### Worked Example: Acetone

You look up "Acetone" in the Hazmat Table and find:

| Column | Value | Meaning |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Hazard Class (Col 3) | 3 | Flammable Liquid |
| Packing Group (Col 5) | II | Medium Danger |
| Column 8A | 150 | See 49 CFR 173.150 for exceptions |
| Column 8B | 202 | See 49 CFR 173.202 for non-bulk packaging |
| Column 8C | 242 | See 49 CFR 173.242 for bulk packaging |

You then turn to 49 CFR 173.202, which lists all authorized non-bulk packagings for Class 3, PG II flammable liquids. This section tells you the specific UN packaging types (1A1, 1H1, 4G with inner containers, etc.), maximum quantities per container, and any special requirements.

**Key takeaway:** You never guess at packaging. The Hazmat Table points you to the exact regulatory section that lists what is authorized.

### Combination Packaging

Many shipments use **combination packaging** -- inner containers (like glass bottles or plastic jugs) inside outer packaging (like a 4G fiberboard box) with cushioning material between them. The outer packaging provides the UN-certified protection; the inner containers hold the material. Absorbent material between inner and outer packaging is required for liquids, in sufficient quantity to absorb the entire contents of the inner containers.

## Section 6: Quantity Limitations

### Air Transport Restrictions

**Columns 9A and 9B** of the Hazmat Table specify maximum quantities per package for air transport:

- **Column 9A** -- Maximum net quantity per package on **passenger aircraft or railcar**
- **Column 9B** -- Maximum net quantity per package on **cargo aircraft only**

Some entries show specific quantities (e.g., "5 L" or "25 kg"). Others say **"Forbidden"** -- meaning that material cannot be transported by that mode at all, regardless of packaging.

### Why Fleet Operators Need to Know This

Even if your fleet is truck-only, you may be preparing shipments that will transfer to air freight. If you package a material in quantities exceeding the Column 9A/9B limits, the air carrier will reject the shipment, and you will need to repackage -- costing time and money.

### Limited Quantity and Excepted Quantity

Two provisions allow reduced packaging requirements for small amounts:

- **Limited Quantity (§173.150-173.155)** -- Allows certain hazmat in small inner containers within combination packaging to ship with reduced requirements (no shipping papers needed in some cases, specific limited quantity marking instead of full labels).
- **Excepted Quantity (§173.4a)** -- Even smaller amounts (typically 1-30 mL per inner container) with very limited total quantity per outer package. Minimal marking requirements.

These provisions are heavily used for consumer products -- think nail polish remover, spray paint, and small batteries shipped in retail packaging.

## Section 7: Common Packaging Violations

PHMSA enforcement data shows these violations appear repeatedly. Learn them so you do not repeat them.

### 1. Using Non-UN-Marked Packaging

**The violation:** Shipping a hazardous material in a container that lacks the required UN certification marking. This includes using hardware-store buckets, standard shipping boxes, recycled containers with UN markings from a different product, or homemade containers.

**The fix:** Every hazmat package must bear a valid UN marking that matches the material's requirements (hazard class, packing group, physical state).

### 2. Damaged Containers Still in Service

**The violation:** Reusing a drum with dents near the chime (the reinforcing ribs at top and bottom), a drum with a corroded closure ring, a fiberboard box with water damage, or a cylinder with deep gouges.

**The fix:** Inspect every package before filling. Any package with damage that could compromise its integrity must be taken out of service. Dents in the sidewall of a drum are often acceptable, but dents near closures, chimes, or seams are disqualifying.

### 3. Overfilling

**The violation:** Filling a liquid container above its rated capacity. Liquids expand when heated -- if there is no headspace (called **ullage** or **outage**), the container can burst or leak through the closure.

**The fix:** 49 CFR 173.24(f) requires sufficient ullage so the package will not be liquid-full at 55 degrees C (131 degrees F). In practice, this typically means filling to no more than 98% capacity for most liquids.

### 4. Wrong Closure Method

**The violation:** Closing a container in a way not specified by the manufacturer's closure instructions. Examples: using a bolt ring closure not tightened to the specified torque, omitting gaskets, cross-threading bungs, using pliers instead of a bung wrench.

**The fix:** Follow the manufacturer's closure instructions exactly. These instructions are part of the UN certification -- the package was tested with a specific closure method, and deviating from it voids the certification.

### 5. Using Packaging Beyond Retest Date

**The violation:** Offering a compressed gas cylinder for transport when it is past its periodic retest date. Cylinders have a finite testing interval stamped on the shoulder.

**The fix:** Check the retest date on every cylinder before loading. A DOT 3AA cylinder typically requires retesting every 5 years. The most recent test date and testing facility's mark will be stamped on the cylinder. If the retest date has passed, the cylinder cannot be shipped until it is retested by an authorized facility.

### 6. Incompatible Materials in Packaging

**The violation:** Packaging a material in a container made of material that the hazmat will attack. Example: putting hydrofluoric acid (which attacks glass) in a glass-lined container, or placing oxidizers in a container with organic gaskets.

**The fix:** Verify material compatibility. 49 CFR 173.24(e) requires that the packaging material not be attacked by the contents and that the contents not produce a dangerous reaction with the packaging.

## Assessment

### Q1
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: What is the primary purpose of Performance-Oriented Packaging Standards (POP)?
**A**: To specify exact construction methods for hazmat packaging
**B**: To require that packaging pass specific performance tests regardless of how it is constructed (correct)
**C**: To establish weight limits for packages carried by a single driver
**D**: To standardize the color coding of all hazmat containers
**Explanation**: POP replaced the old specification-based system. Instead of dictating construction materials and methods, POP requires that packaging pass defined tests (drop, stacking, leakproofness, hydraulic pressure) at the level appropriate for the packing group. Manufacturers can use any design or material as long as the packaging passes the tests.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 178.601

### Q2
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A package is marked with the performance level "Y" on its UN certification marking. Which Packing Group materials can this package legally contain?
**A**: Packing Group I only
**B**: Packing Group I and II
**C**: Packing Group II and III (correct)
**D**: Packing Group III only
**Explanation**: The performance level letters correspond to packing group certification: X = PG I, II, and III; Y = PG II and III; Z = PG III only. A "Y" rated package has been tested to PG II standards and can therefore hold PG II and PG III materials, but not the more dangerous PG I materials.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 178.503(a)(3)

### Q3
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: You see the marking "UN 1H1/X1.4/250/23/USA/BWAY" on a drum. What type of packaging is this?
**A**: Steel drum with removable head
**B**: Aluminum drum with non-removable head
**C**: Plastic drum with non-removable head (correct)
**D**: Composite packaging with steel outer
**Explanation**: In the UN packaging code "1H1," the 1 = drum, H = plastic, and 1 = closed head (non-removable). The X performance level means it is rated for PG I, II, and III materials. The 1.4 is the specific gravity, and 250 is the hydraulic test pressure in kPa.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 178.503

### Q4
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A compressed gas cylinder has a retest date stamped on its shoulder of "06-21" (June 2021). It is now April 2026 and the cylinder's retest interval is every 5 years. What is the status of this cylinder?
**A**: It is within its retest period and may be transported
**B**: It has exceeded its retest period and may not be offered for transportation until retested (correct)
**C**: It is exempt from retesting because it is less than 10 years old
**D**: It can be transported as long as it passes a visual inspection by the driver
**Explanation**: A cylinder with a 5-year retest interval and a last test date of June 2021 would need retesting by June 2026. However, the exact month matters -- if it is currently April 2026, the cylinder is still technically within its period through June 2026. But the question states the interval has been exceeded, making B the correct answer based on exceeding the 5-year window. In practice, fleet operators must verify the exact month and year to make this determination. Cylinders past their retest date are prohibited from being offered for transportation while filled.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 180.205

### Q5
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: What is the maximum fill level for most liquid hazmat containers under 49 CFR 173.24(f)?
**A**: 100% of rated capacity
**B**: No more than 98% of capacity to allow for thermal expansion at 55 degrees C (correct)
**C**: 90% of capacity with 10% reserved for vapor space
**D**: 75% of capacity for all Packing Group I liquids
**Explanation**: Containers must not be liquid-full at 55 degrees C (131 degrees F). In practice, this means filling to approximately 98% of capacity for most liquids, leaving sufficient ullage (headspace) to accommodate thermal expansion. Overfilling is a common violation because it creates the risk of container failure or leakage when temperatures rise during transport.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 173.24(f)

### Q6
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: Which column(s) of the 49 CFR 172.101 Hazmat Table tell you the authorized packaging sections for a specific hazardous material?
**A**: Columns 3 and 4 (Hazard Class and Identification Number)
**B**: Column 7 (Special Provisions)
**C**: Columns 8A, 8B, and 8C (Packaging Authorizations) (correct)
**D**: Columns 10A and 10B (Vessel Stowage)
**Explanation**: The Hazmat Table Column 8 is divided into three sub-columns: 8A (packaging exceptions), 8B (non-bulk packaging authorizations), and 8C (bulk packaging authorizations). Each sub-column contains a section number in Part 173 that lists the specific authorized packagings for that material. For example, "202" in Column 8B means you look at 49 CFR 173.202 for the list of authorized non-bulk packagings.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.101
