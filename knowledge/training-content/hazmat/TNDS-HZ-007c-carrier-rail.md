# Carrier Requirements: Rail

## Learning Objectives
- Describe the scale and types of hazmat transported by rail
- Explain train placement rules for hazmat rail cars under 49 CFR 174.81-85
- Identify speed restrictions that apply to trains carrying hazardous materials
- Describe safe switching operations for placarded cars
- Outline tank car inspection requirements
- Explain how rail hazmat incidents differ from highway incidents
- Recognize how rail hazmat rules affect highway fleet operators at transload and intermodal facilities

## Section 1: Rail Transport Overview

Rail is the workhorse of bulk hazmat transportation in the United States. Railroads carry approximately 1.7 million carloads of hazardous materials per year — roughly 4,600 carloads every single day. The materials moving by rail include many of the most dangerous substances in commerce: chlorine for water treatment, anhydrous ammonia for agriculture, liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) for heating, crude oil, ethanol, sulfuric acid, and sodium hydroxide.

The scale of rail hazmat transport is what sets it apart from every other mode. A single railroad tank car can hold between **20,000 and 34,500 gallons** depending on its specification. That is roughly the equivalent of four to six highway cargo tank trucks in a single railcar. A unit train — a train composed entirely of a single commodity — can consist of 100 or more tank cars. A 100-car unit train of crude oil holds approximately 3 million gallons. When something goes wrong with a rail hazmat shipment, the quantities involved can dwarf anything that occurs on the highway.

### The Incidents That Changed the Rules

Rail hazmat incidents tend to be catastrophic because of the volume involved. These are some of the events that shaped current regulations:

**Lac-Megantic, Quebec (2013)**: A 72-car unit train of Bakken crude oil rolled unattended downhill into the town of Lac-Megantic. Multiple tank cars derailed and ruptured, causing a massive fireball that destroyed 30 buildings in the downtown core and killed 47 people. This disaster led to sweeping changes in tank car design standards, speed restrictions for high-hazard flammable trains, and braking system requirements.

**Graniteville, South Carolina (2005)**: A freight train collided with a parked train in a rail yard, puncturing a tank car carrying chlorine gas (Division 2.3, Toxic by Inhalation). The release of 11,500 gallons of chlorine killed 9 people, injured 554, and forced the evacuation of 5,400 residents within a one-mile radius. Emergency responders could not approach the car for days.

**East Palestine, Ohio (2023)**: A Norfolk Southern freight train carrying vinyl chloride, butyl acrylate, and other hazardous materials derailed. Authorities conducted a controlled burn of vinyl chloride from five tank cars to prevent an uncontrolled explosion, sending a massive plume of black smoke over the community and contaminating soil and waterways.

Each of these incidents reinforced the reality that rail hazmat safety is not about one tank car — it is about the aggregate risk of dozens or hundreds of tank cars moving in a single train.

## Section 2: Train Placement Rules (174.81-85)

The physical position of hazmat cars within a train is regulated because the location of a railcar affects its risk profile in a derailment. The rules in 49 CFR 174.81 through 174.85 establish where hazmat cars can and cannot be placed.

### Buffer Car Requirements

Certain types of hazmat railcars must have **buffer cars** — non-placarded railcars that do not contain hazardous materials — placed between them and other high-risk cars or the locomotive. The buffer car absorbs impact energy in a collision and creates physical separation between incompatible materials.

The general rules for placement include:

- **Placarded cars cannot be placed next to the locomotive** in most cases. There must be at least one non-placarded car (buffer) between any placarded car and an occupied locomotive. The exception is for cars displaying COMBUSTIBLE placards only, which may be placed adjacent to the locomotive.

- **Division 1.1 and 1.2 explosives** (mass explosion hazard and projection hazard) must be placed with the maximum practical separation from the locomotive and from any occupied caboose or crew car. They must also be separated from Division 2.3 (poison gas) and Division 6.1 (poison) cars by at least one buffer car.

- **Division 2.3 Toxic Inhalation Hazard (TIH) materials** — such as chlorine, anhydrous ammonia, and hydrogen fluoride — have some of the most restrictive placement rules. These cars must not be placed next to the locomotive and must be separated from Division 1.1 explosives. Railroads often place TIH cars toward the middle of the train where they are most protected in a derailment.

- **Radioactive materials** (Class 7) require separation distances from occupied spaces and from undeveloped photographic film.

### Why Placement Matters

In a derailment, the forces involved cause cars to jackknife, pile up, and override each other. Cars closest to the point of derailment suffer the most damage. By placing hazmat cars away from the locomotive (which is often near the head of the derailment) and by buffering them from incompatible materials, the rules reduce the chance that a derailment will breach a hazmat car or that two incompatible materials will mix.

**Real-World Scenario**: A railroad is assembling a train that includes 15 loaded tank cars of chlorine (Division 2.3, TIH), 5 cars of Division 1.3 explosives (propellant), and 80 cars of general freight. The train crew cannot simply hook the cars together in any order. The chlorine cars must be separated from the explosives cars by buffer cars. Neither group can be adjacent to the locomotive. The railroad's operating rules and the placement requirements of 174.81-85 dictate a specific marshaling order. Getting this wrong is a federal violation and a safety hazard.

## Section 3: Speed Restrictions

Speed is a critical factor in derailment severity. The kinetic energy involved in a derailment increases with the square of the speed — a train traveling at 50 mph has four times the derailment energy of one traveling at 25 mph. For this reason, trains carrying certain hazardous materials face mandatory speed limits that are lower than the limits for general freight trains.

### Key Speed Rules

- **High-Hazard Flammable Trains (HHFT)**: A train with 20 or more loaded tank cars of Class 3 flammable liquids in a continuous block, or 35 or more such cars anywhere in the train, is classified as a High-Hazard Flammable Train. HHFTs are subject to a maximum speed of **50 mph** nationwide, reduced to **40 mph** within high-threat urban areas (HTUAs) unless the train is equipped with electronically controlled pneumatic (ECP) brakes.

- **Toxic Inhalation Hazard (TIH) materials**: Trains carrying TIH materials in key trains (certain configurations) are subject to speed restrictions, typically **50 mph** or less, depending on railroad operating rules and the specific route.

- **Track condition factors**: Speed restrictions also depend on the class of track. A train carrying hazmat on Class 3 track (maximum 40 mph for freight) cannot exceed the track speed limit regardless of any higher general speed limit. Railroads must ensure their hazmat trains operate on track that is in good condition relative to the speed being used.

- **Railroad operating rules**: Individual railroads may impose their own speed restrictions for hazmat trains that are more restrictive than federal requirements. For example, a railroad may limit all TIH trains to 40 mph on a specific subdivision based on curvature, grade, or community exposure.

### Practical Impact

Speed restrictions mean hazmat trains take longer to reach their destinations. This has economic implications — slower trains tie up track capacity and delay other traffic — but the tradeoff is reduced derailment risk. After Lac-Megantic and East Palestine, the regulatory and public pressure for stricter speed limits on hazmat trains has only increased.

## Section 4: Switching Operations (174.82-83)

Switching is the process of assembling and disassembling trains in rail yards — coupling and uncoupling cars, moving them between tracks, and building trains for departure. Switching is one of the most hazardous activities in railroading because it involves moving cars at close quarters with workers on the ground guiding movements.

### Rules for Handling Placarded Cars During Switching

49 CFR 174.82 and 174.83 establish specific requirements:

- **No rough handling.** Placarded hazmat cars must not be subjected to "humping" or "kicking." Humping is the practice of pushing a car over a hump (a small hill) in the yard and letting gravity roll it down to the correct track, where it couples to other cars with a hard impact. Kicking is a similar practice where a car is given a hard shove and allowed to roll freely into a track. Both practices create violent impacts that can damage tank car fittings, valves, and shells. Placarded cars must be **flat-switched** — moved at controlled speed with the locomotive connected the entire time.

- **Speed limits during coupling.** When coupling to a placarded car, the movement must be made at **no more than 4 mph** and preferably slower. The goal is to minimize the impact force on the tank car. A hard coupling impact can crack welds, damage pressure relief valves, or unseat fittings.

- **Brakes must be set.** Before a placarded car is left standing on a track, its hand brakes must be applied. The car must not be left unattended on a grade where it could roll.

- **Attendance and monitoring.** Placarded cars in a switching yard must not be left on an open main track. They must be placed on a designated track with appropriate derails or other physical protections to prevent uncontrolled movement.

**Real-World Scenario**: A yard crew needs to add a tank car of anhydrous ammonia (Division 2.3, TIH) to an outbound train. The yard has a hump classification system, but the TIH car cannot go over the hump. Instead, the crew must pull the car with a yard locomotive, flat-switch it to the correct track, and couple it gently at no more than 4 mph. The crew member on the ground signals the engineer to slow down during the coupling move. This process takes more time than humping, but the alternative — a ruptured ammonia car in a populated rail yard — is not acceptable.

## Section 5: Inspection Requirements

Railroad tank cars carrying hazmat must be inspected at multiple points during their lifecycle and during each trip.

### Pre-Trip Inspection

Before a loaded tank car departs, it must be inspected for:

- **Leaks** — Any visible liquid dripping from valves, fittings, or the shell is a rejection criterion. The car must be repaired or the material transferred before the car moves.
- **Valve condition** — All valves (liquid, vapor, bottom outlet where applicable) must be properly closed and secured. Protective housings and manway covers must be in place.
- **Shell damage** — Dents, cracks, gouges, and signs of corrosion on the tank car shell must be evaluated. Certain types of damage (especially near welds or in the head area) are grounds for removing the car from service.
- **Pressure relief devices** — The safety valve or pressure relief device must be present, properly set, and not leaking. A missing or malfunctioning relief device means the car cannot transport pressurized materials.
- **Placards and markings** — The car must display the correct placards for the material being carried, and all required markings (reporting marks, stenciled tank specification, retest dates) must be legible.

### En Route and Arrival Inspection

When a train stops at an intermediate point (crew change, fuel stop, or interchange with another railroad), hazmat cars should be checked for leaks or signs of distress. At the destination, the car is inspected again before unloading.

### Periodic Tank Car Qualification

Beyond trip-by-trip inspections, tank cars must undergo periodic testing and requalification under 49 CFR Part 180. This includes:

- **Visual inspection** of the tank shell, heads, fittings, and protective structures at prescribed intervals (typically every 5 or 10 years depending on the car's specification).
- **Thickness testing** of the shell using ultrasonic or other methods to ensure corrosion has not reduced the wall thickness below minimum specifications.
- **Pressure testing** (hydrostatic or pneumatic) to confirm the tank can withstand its rated pressure.
- **Valve and fitting testing** to ensure pressure relief devices are set correctly and all closures are tight.

A tank car that fails qualification testing must be repaired or retired from hazmat service.

## Section 6: Incident Response for Rail

When a rail hazmat incident occurs, it presents challenges that are fundamentally different from a highway incident.

### Scale

A single highway cargo tank holds roughly 5,000 to 9,000 gallons. A single railroad tank car holds 20,000 to 34,500 gallons. A unit train can involve 100+ cars. The sheer volume of material that can be released in a rail derailment is staggering. The East Palestine derailment involved approximately 100,000 gallons of vinyl chloride alone.

### Access Difficulties

Derailments often occur in locations with limited road access. Unlike a highway spill where responders can drive right up to the scene, a rail derailment may be in a ravine, on a bridge, or in a remote area accessible only by rail or on foot. Heavy cranes, vacuum trucks, and response equipment may need to be moved in by rail or helicopter.

### Cascading Risks

When one car derails and ruptures, the resulting fire or explosion can damage adjacent cars. A pool fire under a pressurized tank car can heat the liquid inside, increasing the internal pressure until the car fails catastrophically in a **BLEVE (Boiling Liquid Expanding Vapor Explosion)**. A single BLEVE from an LPG car can throw fragments over a half-mile radius and produce a fireball 300 feet in diameter.

### The ERG and Isolation Zones

The Emergency Response Guidebook (ERG 2024) is critical for initial rail incident response. The orange pages provide material-specific response guidance. The green pages provide **initial isolation and protective action distances** for TIH materials. These distances tell first responders how far to evacuate or shelter-in-place around a release.

For a chlorine release from a rail car, the ERG recommends an initial isolation distance of 330 feet in all directions and a protective action distance of up to 2.9 miles downwind during a large nighttime spill. These numbers are not theoretical — the Graniteville chlorine release required evacuation of a one-mile radius and was lethal within 1,500 feet of the car.

### Railroad Hazmat Response Resources

The major Class I railroads (BNSF, Union Pacific, CSX, Norfolk Southern) maintain their own hazmat response teams, emergency management centers, and specialized equipment. They also participate in mutual aid agreements such as CHEMTREC and regional hazmat response cooperatives. However, first response typically falls to local fire departments, who may arrive on scene before railroad resources. This makes ERG training for first responders at rail crossings and rail-adjacent communities critically important.

**Real-World Scenario**: A 15-car section of a train carrying mixed hazmat derails at 2 AM near a small town. Three tank cars of ethanol (Class 3) are on fire. One car of chlorine (Division 2.3) is on its side but appears intact. Local fire fighters arrive first. Using the ERG, they identify the chlorine car by its placard and reporting marks, look up the initial isolation distance (1,000 feet for a large spill), and establish a perimeter. They do not attempt to fight the ethanol fire near the chlorine car — they evacuate the downwind area and wait for the railroad's hazmat team, who arrive four hours later with specialized monitoring equipment and plugging kits. The initial ERG-based response by local firefighters likely saved lives by establishing the correct isolation distance before the railroad specialists arrived.

## Section 7: Relevance to Fleet Operators

If your fleet operates on highways, you may wonder why rail hazmat rules matter to you. The answer is that highway and rail operations intersect at multiple points, and understanding rail rules prevents costly mistakes.

### Transload Facilities

A transload facility is a location where material is transferred between rail cars and highway vehicles. Your tanker truck may pull up alongside a railroad tank car to load or unload product. At a transload:

- The material must be properly reclassified and documented for the new mode of transport if any differences apply.
- The highway shipping papers must be prepared correctly — the rail waybill is not a substitute for a highway bill of lading.
- The loading operation must comply with both Part 174 (rail) and Part 177 (highway) requirements. The tank car valves must be properly connected, the truck's ground strap must be attached (for flammable liquids), and both the car and the truck must be attended during the transfer.
- Quantity differences matter. A rail car holding 20,000 gallons of a flammable liquid may feed multiple truck loads. Each truck load needs its own shipping papers and placards.

### Intermodal Terminals

Container-on-flatcar (COFC) and trailer-on-flatcar (TOFC) operations involve moving highway trailers or containers by rail for part of the journey. If your fleet delivers a hazmat container to a rail terminal for intermodal transport, the container must meet both highway and rail standards. Placards must be applied to all four sides of the container (visible whether on a truck or a flatcar). The shipping papers must include the intermodal routing information.

### Rail Yard Pickups and Deliveries

Some fleet operations involve picking up or delivering freight directly at rail yards. If your driver enters a rail yard to pick up a container or deliver a load, the driver must follow the yard's safety rules, which include rail-specific hazmat handling procedures. Drivers should never walk between rail cars, should be aware of train movements, and should verify that any hazmat container they pick up has intact placards and documentation before leaving the yard.

**Real-World Scenario**: Your fleet sends a tanker truck to a transload facility to pick up 5,000 gallons of sodium hydroxide (Class 8, Corrosive) from a rail tank car. The driver arrives and connects the hose to begin loading. After loading, the driver needs to prepare a highway bill of lading listing the proper shipping name, hazard class, UN number, packing group, and quantity — the rail waybill that accompanied the tank car is not a valid highway shipping paper. The driver must also verify that CORROSIVE placards are displayed on all four sides of the tanker truck before departing. Failure to prepare highway-specific documentation at the transload point is a common violation that inspectors specifically look for.

## Key Takeaways
- Rail transports enormous quantities of hazmat — a single tank car holds 20,000-34,500 gallons
- Train placement rules require buffer cars between hazmat cars and locomotives, and between incompatible materials
- Speed restrictions (40-50 mph) apply to high-hazard flammable trains and trains carrying TIH materials
- Placarded cars must not be humped or kicked during switching — flat-switching at 4 mph or less is required
- Rail incidents can involve massive quantities and may require ERG-based isolation zones measured in miles
- Highway fleet operators interact with rail at transload facilities and intermodal terminals, where proper documentation and compliance handoffs are critical
