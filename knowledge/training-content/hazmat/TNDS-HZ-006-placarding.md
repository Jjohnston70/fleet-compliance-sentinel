# TNDS-HZ-006: Placarding Requirements

## Learning Objectives

After completing this module, you will be able to:

- Explain the purpose of placards and who relies on them
- Distinguish between Table 1 and Table 2 placarding requirements
- Apply the 1,001-pound threshold rule correctly
- Determine when the DANGEROUS placard may or may not be used
- Identify subsidiary hazard placarding requirements
- Describe proper placard placement, maintenance, and removal
- Recognize the role of UN/NA identification numbers on placards

---

## 1. What Is Placarding?

Placards are diamond-shaped signs displayed on the outside of transport vehicles and freight containers. They are at least 250 mm (10.8 inches) on each side, printed in bold colors that correspond to hazard classes. Their single purpose is to communicate what kind of hazardous material is inside the vehicle -- quickly and at a distance.

**Who reads placards?** Firefighters, police officers, EMS crews, HAZMAT teams, and the general public. When a truck is involved in a crash or fire, the placard is often the very first piece of information a first responder has to work with. If the placard is wrong, missing, or unreadable, responders may use the wrong approach -- and people can get hurt or killed.

**How placards relate to labels:** Labels are smaller (100 mm / 3.9 inches on a side) and go on individual packages. Placards use the same color scheme and symbol design as labels, but they are scaled up for vehicle-level visibility. You can think of it this way: labels tell the warehouse worker what is in a box; placards tell the firefighter what is on the truck.

**Where placards go:** Federal regulations under 49 CFR 172.516 require placards on all four sides of the vehicle -- front, rear, and both sides. This ensures that no matter what angle a responder approaches from, they can identify the hazard. The placard must be clearly visible, upright in the "square-on-point" diamond orientation, and not obscured by ladders, doors, mud, or equipment.

> **Real-World Scenario:** A tanker carrying gasoline rolls over on an interstate off-ramp at 2:00 AM. The first fire engine arrives within four minutes. The crew cannot see the shipping papers because the cab is crushed. But from 200 feet away, the crew chief reads the red FLAMMABLE placard on the rear of the tanker. That single piece of information tells the crew to keep ignition sources away, set up foam lines instead of water, and establish a larger evacuation perimeter. The placard just drove the entire response strategy before anyone got close to the vehicle.

## 2. Table 1 vs. Table 2 Placards (49 CFR 172.504)

This is the most important concept in placarding. The regulations divide hazardous materials into two tables, and the rules for when you need placards are completely different depending on which table your material falls under.

### Table 1: Placard at ANY Quantity

Table 1 materials are so dangerous that a placard is required no matter how little you carry -- even one package. There is no weight threshold. If it is on the truck, you placard.

Table 1 materials include:

| Hazard Class/Division | Placard Name | Why It Is Table 1 |
|---|---|---|
| Division 1.1 | EXPLOSIVES 1.1 | Mass explosion hazard |
| Division 1.2 | EXPLOSIVES 1.2 | Projection hazard |
| Division 1.3 | EXPLOSIVES 1.3 | Fire, minor blast, or projection hazard |
| Division 2.3 | POISON GAS | Toxic by inhalation -- immediate life threat |
| Division 4.3 | DANGEROUS WHEN WET | Produces flammable or toxic gas on contact with water |
| Division 6.1 (PG I, inhalation hazard) | POISON (INHALATION HAZARD) | Fatal vapors at low concentration |
| Class 7 (Yellow III label only) | RADIOACTIVE | Highest external radiation level for transport |

**Memory aid:** Table 1 materials are the ones where even a small release could cause mass casualties. Explosives blow up. Poison gas kills by breathing. Dangerous when wet reacts violently with something that is everywhere (water). If emergency response goes wrong with these materials, the consequences are catastrophic. That is why there is no quantity exemption.

### Table 2: Placard at 1,001 Pounds or More

Table 2 materials require placarding only when the aggregate gross weight of all Table 2 materials on the vehicle reaches or exceeds 454 kg (1,001 lbs). This is commonly called the "1,001-pound rule."

Table 2 materials include:

| Hazard Class/Division | Placard Name |
|---|---|
| Division 1.4 | EXPLOSIVES 1.4 |
| Division 1.5 | EXPLOSIVES 1.5 |
| Division 1.6 | EXPLOSIVES 1.6 |
| Division 2.1 | FLAMMABLE GAS |
| Division 2.2 | NON-FLAMMABLE GAS |
| Division 4.1 | FLAMMABLE SOLID |
| Division 4.2 | SPONTANEOUSLY COMBUSTIBLE |
| Division 5.1 | OXIDIZER |
| Division 5.2 | ORGANIC PEROXIDE |
| Division 6.1 (other than PG I inhalation) | POISON |
| Class 8 | CORROSIVE |
| Class 9 | CLASS 9 (miscellaneous) |
| ORM-D | None required |

**Key detail: aggregate gross weight.** "Aggregate" means you add up the gross weight (material plus packaging) of ALL Table 2 materials on the vehicle, regardless of hazard class. You do not calculate each class separately for the 1,001-pound threshold.

> **Worked Example:** Your truck picks up the following shipments:
>
> - 400 lbs of flammable liquid (Division 3, Table 2)
> - 300 lbs of corrosive material (Class 8, Table 2)
> - 350 lbs of oxidizer (Division 5.1, Table 2)
>
> **Total aggregate gross weight: 1,050 lbs.** This exceeds 1,001 lbs, so placarding IS required. You need placards for each hazard class present: FLAMMABLE, CORROSIVE, and OXIDIZER -- or you may use the DANGEROUS placard (see Section 3 below).

> **Another Example:** Your truck has only 800 lbs of flammable liquid and nothing else.
>
> 800 lbs is below the 1,001-lb threshold. No placards required for Table 2 materials.

> **Mixed Table Example:** Your truck has 1 package (50 lbs) of Division 2.3 Poison Gas and 500 lbs of flammable liquid.
>
> The Division 2.3 is a Table 1 material -- you MUST placard POISON GAS regardless of quantity. The 500 lbs of flammable liquid is below the 1,001-lb Table 2 threshold on its own -- but because you are already required to placard for the Table 1 material, you should check whether the total Table 2 aggregate triggers additional placarding. In this case, 500 lbs is below 1,001 lbs, so no FLAMMABLE placard is needed. The truck displays POISON GAS placards only.

## 3. The DANGEROUS Placard

The DANGEROUS placard is a white diamond with the word "DANGEROUS" in red. It exists as a convenience for carriers transporting mixed loads of Table 2 materials.

### When You May Use It

You may use a single DANGEROUS placard in place of multiple specific Table 2 placards when:

- The vehicle carries **two or more** Table 2 materials that would require **different** placards, AND
- The **total aggregate gross weight** of all Table 2 materials is 1,001 lbs or more

Instead of displaying FLAMMABLE, CORROSIVE, and OXIDIZER placards (from the example above), you could display a single DANGEROUS placard on all four sides.

### When You Must NOT Use It

You **cannot** use the DANGEROUS placard when:

1. **1,000 kg (2,205 lbs) or more** of a single Table 2 material is loaded at one facility. In that case, you must display the **specific placard** for that material. You may still use DANGEROUS for the other Table 2 materials if they individually fall below 2,205 lbs.

2. **Table 1 materials.** The DANGEROUS placard never replaces Table 1 placards. A Table 1 material always gets its own specific placard.

> **Scenario:** A driver picks up 2,500 lbs of sulfuric acid (Class 8, Corrosive) at Warehouse A, then picks up 200 lbs of paint (Division 3, Flammable) and 300 lbs of bleach (Division 5.1, Oxidizer) at Warehouse B.
>
> - The sulfuric acid exceeds 2,205 lbs loaded at one location, so you **must** display a CORROSIVE placard.
> - For the remaining materials (paint + bleach = 500 lbs), the aggregate is below 1,001 lbs on its own, so additional placards are not required for those materials.
> - Final placarding: CORROSIVE on all four sides.

> **Another Scenario:** A driver picks up 600 lbs of paint thinner (Flammable), 500 lbs of muriatic acid (Corrosive), and 400 lbs of calcium hypochlorite (Oxidizer) -- all from the same warehouse.
>
> - Total aggregate: 1,500 lbs -- exceeds 1,001 lbs, so placarding is required.
> - No single material exceeds 2,205 lbs at one loading point.
> - The driver may use the DANGEROUS placard on all four sides instead of displaying FLAMMABLE, CORROSIVE, and OXIDIZER.

## 4. Subsidiary Hazard Placards

Some hazardous materials pose more than one type of hazard. The primary hazard determines the main placard, but if the material has a **subsidiary hazard**, an additional placard may be required.

**How to know:** The shipping papers and the Hazardous Materials Table (49 CFR 172.101) list subsidiary hazards. If a subsidiary placard is required, it will be indicated in Column 6 of the Hazardous Materials Table.

**Common examples:**

- **Methyl isocyanate** is primarily a Division 6.1 Poison but also has a Division 3 Flammable subsidiary hazard. The vehicle displays both POISON and FLAMMABLE placards.
- **Chlorine** is Division 2.3 Poison Gas with a Division 5.1 Oxidizer and Division 8 Corrosive subsidiary hazard. The vehicle displays POISON GAS, OXIDIZER, and CORROSIVE placards.

**Rule of thumb:** When in doubt, check the shipping papers. If a subsidiary hazard label is required on the package, the corresponding placard is required on the vehicle.

## 5. Placard Placement and Specifications

Federal regulations are specific about how placards must be displayed (49 CFR 172.516):

- **All four sides:** Front, rear, left side, right side. No exceptions.
- **Square-on-point orientation:** The diamond must stand on one corner, not flat on a side.
- **Clear and visible:** Placards must be readable from the direction they face. Nothing can obscure them -- no mud, no cargo straps over them, no doors swung open in front of them during loading.
- **Securely attached:** Placards must be held in holders or otherwise attached so they do not blow off at highway speeds. Wire, clips, or approved placard holders are standard.
- **Not on doors:** If a placard is mounted on a door or panel that swings open during loading/unloading, the placard is not visible while the door is open. Use a location that remains visible at all times, or have a procedure to ensure placards are displayed when the vehicle is in transit.
- **Condition:** Placards must not be faded, torn, or damaged to the point of being illegible. Replace worn placards.
- **Color and size:** Must conform to the design specifications in 49 CFR 172.519 and 172.521. Minimum size is 250 mm (10.8 inches) on each side. Colors must match the regulatory standards (red for flammable, green for non-flammable gas, yellow for oxidizer, white for poison, etc.).

> **Practical Tip:** Many fleets use flip-placard holders mounted permanently on all four sides of their trailers. The driver flips to the correct placard for the current load. This is fast, keeps the placard secure, and avoids the problem of placards blowing off the truck at 65 mph.

## 6. When Placards Must Be Removed

This is a point many drivers overlook, and inspectors look for it.

**The rule (49 CFR 172.502):** No person may display a placard on a vehicle unless the vehicle contains the hazardous material identified by that placard, or residue of that material. Once the hazmat cargo is unloaded and the vehicle is clean, placards must be removed or covered.

**Why it matters:**

- **False alarm response:** If a truck displaying FLAMMABLE placards is involved in a fender-bender but is actually carrying auto parts, the fire department may roll a full HAZMAT team, shut down the highway, and evacuate nearby buildings -- all for nothing. This wastes emergency resources and costs money.
- **Legal violation:** Displaying incorrect placards is a federal violation under 49 CFR 172.502(a). Fines apply.
- **Tunnel and bridge restrictions:** A truck displaying hazmat placards may be refused entry to tunnels or bridges that restrict hazmat vehicles. If the truck is actually empty, the driver has unnecessarily limited their routing options.

> **Best Practice:** Make placard removal part of your post-unloading checklist. Walk around the vehicle after unloading and flip, remove, or cover all placards. Some carriers require a photo of the bare (unplacarded) vehicle as documentation.

## 7. UN/NA Number Display

Every hazardous material has a four-digit identification number assigned by the United Nations (UN) or North America (NA). This number appears in the ERG and helps responders identify the exact material, not just the hazard class.

**Display options (49 CFR 172.332-336):**

1. **On the placard itself:** The four-digit number is printed in the center of the placard, replacing the hazard class number. This is the most common method for bulk shipments.
2. **On an orange panel:** A rectangular orange panel (at least 160 mm high by 400 mm wide) displaying the four-digit number is placed adjacent to the placard. This method is often used when a vehicle carries multiple materials.

**When required:**

- Bulk packaging (capacity greater than 450 L / 119 gallons for liquids, or greater than 400 kg / 882 lbs for solids)
- Cargo tanks, tank cars, and portable tanks
- Certain non-bulk shipments of hazmat in quantities requiring placards

> **Example:** A cargo tank carrying diesel fuel displays a red FLAMMABLE placard with "1993" (the UN number for diesel fuel/fuel oil) printed in the center on all four sides. When a responder sees "1993," they look it up in the ERG and find specific guidance for diesel fuel spills, including recommended isolation distances and firefighting procedures.

> **Scenario:** A flatbed truck carries 20 drums of two different chemicals -- UN 1203 (gasoline) and UN 1830 (sulfuric acid). Rather than printing UN numbers on the placards, the carrier uses FLAMMABLE and CORROSIVE placards plus separate orange panels displaying "1203" and "1830" on the appropriate sides.

## 8. Common Placard Mistakes

These are the errors that show up repeatedly in FMCSA roadside inspections and internal safety audits:

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Forgetting to remove old placards | Driver unloads hazmat, picks up non-hazmat load, does not walk around vehicle | False emergency response; federal violation |
| Wrong Table 1 vs. Table 2 determination | Driver does not check shipping papers carefully; assumes all materials follow the 1,001-lb rule | Traveling without required placard (Table 1 material); out-of-service violation |
| Using DANGEROUS when a specific placard is required | Driver does not realize that 2,205 lbs of one material from one facility requires a specific placard | Federal violation; placard does not communicate correct hazard to responders |
| Damaged or illegible placards | Weather, road grime, age | Treated as missing placard during inspection |
| Placard on swinging door | Placard mounted on trailer door that is open during loading | Not visible during operations; inspection violation |
| Missing one side | Placard fell off or was never placed on one of the four required sides | Out-of-service violation at inspection |
| Wrong placard for the material | Mix-up between similar-looking placards (e.g., OXIDIZER vs. ORGANIC PEROXIDE) | Wrong emergency response; potential injury |

> **Cost of Getting It Wrong:** An out-of-service order for a placarding violation means the truck sits on the side of the road until the violation is corrected. The driver loses time, the load is delayed, and the carrier gets a negative mark on its CSA BASIC scores. Repeat violations can lead to increased inspections and potential operating authority consequences.

## Assessment

### Q1
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A vehicle is carrying one package weighing 25 lbs of Division 2.3 (Poison Gas). Is placarding required?
**A**: No, because the total weight is below the 1,001-lb threshold
**B**: Yes, because Division 2.3 is a Table 1 material requiring placarding at any quantity (correct)
**C**: Yes, but only if the vehicle is traveling on an interstate highway
**D**: No, because a single package does not trigger placarding requirements
**Explanation**: Division 2.3 (Poison Gas) is listed on Table 1 in 49 CFR 172.504. Table 1 materials require placarding regardless of quantity -- there is no weight threshold. Even one small package requires the POISON GAS placard on all four sides.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.504(e), Table 1

---

### Q2
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A truck is carrying 600 lbs of flammable liquid (Table 2) and 500 lbs of corrosive material (Table 2). What placarding is required?
**A**: No placards required because neither material individually exceeds 1,001 lbs
**B**: FLAMMABLE placards only, since flammable liquid is the greater quantity
**C**: FLAMMABLE and CORROSIVE placards, or a single DANGEROUS placard, because the aggregate exceeds 1,001 lbs (correct)
**D**: DANGEROUS placard only, because the driver must always use DANGEROUS for mixed loads
**Explanation**: The 1,001-lb threshold is based on the aggregate gross weight of all Table 2 materials, not the weight of each individual class. 600 + 500 = 1,100 lbs, which exceeds 1,001 lbs. The driver must display placards for each hazard class present (FLAMMABLE and CORROSIVE), or may substitute a single DANGEROUS placard since neither material reaches 2,205 lbs from one loading point.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.504(b), Table 2

### Q3
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A driver picks up 2,500 lbs of Class 8 Corrosive material at one warehouse and then picks up 400 lbs of Division 5.1 Oxidizer at a second warehouse. Can the driver use a single DANGEROUS placard for the entire load?
**A**: Yes, because the truck carries two different Table 2 materials
**B**: No, because the corrosive material exceeds 2,205 lbs loaded at one facility, requiring a specific CORROSIVE placard (correct)
**C**: Yes, because the DANGEROUS placard replaces all Table 2 placards regardless of weight
**D**: No, because the DANGEROUS placard can only be used for Table 1 materials
**Explanation**: When 1,000 kg (2,205 lbs) or more of a single Table 2 material is loaded at one facility, the specific placard for that material must be displayed. The DANGEROUS placard cannot replace it. The 2,500 lbs of corrosive material loaded at Warehouse A exceeds this threshold, so a CORROSIVE placard is mandatory.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.504(b)(2)

### Q4
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: How many sides of a transport vehicle must display placards?
**A**: Two sides (front and rear only)
**B**: Three sides (rear and both sides)
**C**: Four sides (front, rear, and both sides) (correct)
**D**: One side (whichever side faces traffic)
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 172.516, placards must be displayed on each end (front and rear) and on each side of the vehicle. This ensures that emergency responders can identify the hazard from any direction of approach.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.516(c)(1)

### Q5
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: After unloading all hazardous materials and cleaning the vehicle, a driver notices the FLAMMABLE placards are still displayed. What should the driver do?
**A**: Leave them in place since they will be needed for the next hazmat load
**B**: Remove or cover the placards before operating the vehicle (correct)
**C**: Leave them in place because removing placards is the shipper's responsibility
**D**: Cover only the front and rear placards, leaving the side placards visible
**Explanation**: 49 CFR 172.502(a) prohibits displaying a placard on a vehicle that does not contain the corresponding hazardous material (or its residue). Displaying incorrect placards can trigger unnecessary emergency response, restrict the vehicle from tunnels and bridges, and is a federal violation. All four placards must be removed or covered.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.502(a)

### Q6
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: What is the minimum dimension for each side of a standard hazmat placard?
**A**: 100 mm (3.9 inches)
**B**: 250 mm (10.8 inches) (correct)
**C**: 300 mm (11.8 inches)
**D**: 200 mm (7.9 inches)
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 172.519(c), each side of a placard must measure at least 250 mm (10.8 inches). This is distinct from hazmat labels, which are 100 mm (3.9 inches) on a side. The larger size ensures placards are readable from a distance by emergency responders.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.519(c)
