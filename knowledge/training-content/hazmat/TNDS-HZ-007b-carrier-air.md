# Carrier Requirements: Air

## Learning Objectives
- Explain why air transport has the strictest hazmat rules of any mode
- Identify materials that are forbidden on passenger and cargo aircraft
- Interpret Column 9A and 9B of the Hazardous Materials Table for air quantity limitations
- Describe packaging requirements specific to air transport
- Complete or verify a Shipper's Declaration for Dangerous Goods
- Apply current lithium battery shipping rules
- Recognize how air transport restrictions affect highway fleet operators

## Section 1: Why Air Transport Has the Strictest Rules

Of all transportation modes, air transport imposes the most restrictive rules on hazardous materials. There are three reasons for this, and each one is non-negotiable.

**Altitude changes everything.** A commercial aircraft cruises at 30,000 to 40,000 feet, where cabin pressure is maintained at the equivalent of roughly 6,000 to 8,000 feet altitude. Cargo holds may be pressurized to similar levels, but the pressure is still significantly lower than at sea level. A container that is perfectly sealed at ground level can leak, bulge, or rupture at altitude because the reduced external pressure allows internal pressure to push outward. Liquids expand. Gases become more volatile. Pressure relief devices may activate when they would not have activated on the ground.

**Temperature swings are extreme.** Outside temperatures at cruise altitude can reach minus 60 degrees Fahrenheit. The cargo hold temperature depends on the aircraft type — some are heated, some are not. Materials that are stable at room temperature may become unstable at these extremes. Aerosol cans can rupture. Chemical reactions can accelerate or slow unpredictably.

**You cannot pull over at 35,000 feet.** On a highway, if a drum starts leaking, the driver can pull to the shoulder, call hazmat response, and isolate the area. On a rail car, the train can stop. On a ship, the crew can access the deck. In an aircraft, the options are limited to whatever the crew can do in a pressurized metal tube traveling at 500 miles per hour. A fire in the cargo hold of an aircraft is one of the most dangerous scenarios in commercial aviation. There is no "exit to the right."

**History proves the point.** On May 11, 1996, ValuJet Flight 592 crashed into the Florida Everglades, killing all 110 people on board. The cause was improperly packaged chemical oxygen generators — hazmat that should never have been on that aircraft — that ignited in the cargo hold. The generators were not classified, not labeled, and not documented on shipping papers. The airline did not know they were on board. That single incident led to sweeping changes in air cargo hazmat regulations. More recently, lithium battery fires in cargo holds have caused multiple aircraft losses, including UPS Flight 6 in Dubai (2010) and Asiana Flight 991 (2011). These are not theoretical risks.

## Section 2: What Is Forbidden on Aircraft

Many hazardous materials that travel safely by highway, rail, or vessel every day are completely forbidden on aircraft. The Hazardous Materials Table in 49 CFR 172.101 tells you what is and is not allowed through two critical columns:

**Column 9A — Passenger Aircraft.** This column shows the maximum net quantity per package allowed on a passenger-carrying aircraft. If the entry says "Forbidden," the material cannot fly on any aircraft carrying passengers — period. No exceptions, no special permits for small quantities.

**Column 9B — Cargo Aircraft Only.** This column shows the maximum net quantity per package allowed on cargo-only aircraft. If this column also says "Forbidden," the material cannot fly at all, on any type of aircraft.

### Materials That Are Always Forbidden by Air

Certain categories of materials are never permitted on aircraft regardless of quantity:

- **Division 1.1 and 1.2 explosives** (mass explosion and projection hazards) — You cannot ship dynamite, detonators, or military ordnance by air through normal commercial channels.
- **Division 6.2 Category A infectious substances** in quantities exceeding the limits specified in the ICAO Technical Instructions — Ebola virus samples, for example, follow an extremely restricted set of air transport rules.
- **Class 7 radioactive materials** exceeding transport index limits for aircraft.
- **Magnetized materials** with a field strength that could affect aircraft instruments.
- Any material that is "Forbidden" in both Column 9A and Column 9B of the Hazmat Table.

### The "Cargo Aircraft Only" Label

When a material is forbidden on passenger aircraft (Column 9A says "Forbidden") but allowed on cargo aircraft (Column 9B shows a quantity), the package must bear a **"Cargo Aircraft Only"** label. This is a distinctive orange-and-white label that tells everyone in the handling chain: this package cannot go on any aircraft that carries passengers.

**Real-World Scenario**: Your fleet picks up 20 drums of a Class 3 flammable liquid from a chemical plant and delivers them to an air freight forwarder at the airport. You look at the Hazmat Table and see that Column 9A says "Forbidden" but Column 9B says "60 L." This means each drum (up to 60 liters net) can fly on a cargo-only aircraft but not on a passenger flight. The shipper must apply the Cargo Aircraft Only label to every drum, and the air carrier must ensure those drums never end up on a passenger flight. If your driver notices the labels are missing when picking up the load, that is a problem that needs to be resolved before the cargo moves.

## Section 3: Quantity Limitations

Even when a material is permitted on aircraft, the allowed quantities per package are dramatically lower than what you would handle on a highway shipment.

### Passenger Aircraft Limits (Column 9A)

Typical limits for passenger aircraft are measured in small quantities — often 1 liter, 5 liters, or 25 kilograms per package. Compare that to highway transport, where a single cargo tank can carry 9,000 gallons of the same material. The air limits exist because any incident on a passenger aircraft puts hundreds of lives at immediate risk.

**Example**: Acetone (UN1090, Class 3, Packing Group II) has a Column 9A limit of 5 liters per package on a passenger aircraft. On a highway, you can haul a 5,500-gallon tanker of acetone with proper placarding and documentation. The difference is more than three orders of magnitude.

### Cargo Aircraft Limits (Column 9B)

Cargo aircraft limits are more generous than passenger limits but still far below highway quantities. Typical limits range from 60 liters to 220 liters for liquids, and 25 kilograms to 200 kilograms for solids.

### How Limits Apply in Practice

The quantity limits are **per package**, not per shipment. You can ship multiple packages on the same aircraft, each at or below the per-package limit, subject to the aircraft operator's own policies and any aggregate quantity restrictions. However, you cannot put 10 liters of a liquid into one package when the limit is 5 liters and call it compliant — the limit applies to each individual package.

**Practical Tip for Fleet Operators**: If your company ships hazmat that eventually moves by air, you need to package it to air standards from the start. Repackaging at the airport is expensive, time-consuming, and introduces additional handling risk. Build the air quantity limits into your packaging process at the origin.

## Section 4: Accessibility Requirements

Certain hazardous materials loaded on aircraft must remain **accessible to the crew** during flight. This means the cargo must be loaded in a position where a crew member can physically reach it, inspect it, and if necessary, apply fire suppression or separate it from other cargo.

On passenger aircraft, hazmat that is permitted in the cabin or in accessible cargo compartments must be stowed so that a crew member can monitor it. Materials classified as toxic or corrosive that are permitted in limited quantities, for example, must not be buried behind tons of other freight where no one can reach them if a leak develops.

On cargo-only aircraft, the accessibility requirement is relaxed for most materials because the entire cargo area is effectively accessible (there is no passenger cabin to protect). However, the operator must still ensure that the hazmat is loaded in a way that allows for identification and response if an incident occurs during flight.

**Why This Matters**: If you are loading a container or unit load device (ULD) that will be placed on an aircraft, the placement of hazmat packages within that container matters. Hazmat should be loaded last (so it is accessible first) or positioned near the container door, not buried in the back behind 4,000 pounds of general freight.

## Section 5: Packaging Requirements Specific to Air

Air transport packaging requirements go beyond what is required for surface transport. PHMSA and the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) both impose additional requirements.

### Inner Packaging Requirements

Liquids shipped by air must have inner packagings — individual bottles, cans, or containers — inside an outer packaging. The inner packagings must be oriented with closures upward and secured against movement. This prevents a single point of failure: if the outer box is damaged, the inner container still contains the liquid.

### Absorbent Material

Packages containing liquid hazmat for air transport must include enough absorbent material to absorb the entire contents of the inner packaging. If a 1-liter bottle of a Class 3 flammable liquid breaks inside the outer package, the absorbent material must be able to soak up all 1 liter so that nothing leaks out of the outer packaging. This is not optional — it is a regulatory requirement.

### Pressure Differential Testing

Inner packagings for liquids must withstand a pressure differential equivalent to the altitude change during flight. The standard test is either a pressure differential of not less than 95 kPa (approximately 14 psi) or the internal pressure at 55 degrees Celsius (131 degrees Fahrenheit), whichever is greater. This ensures the container will not burst when the aircraft climbs to cruising altitude and the cargo hold pressure drops.

### Closure Requirements

All closures (caps, lids, valves) must be secured against loosening due to vibration. This typically means threaded closures must be torqued to specification and may need secondary closure devices (tape, wire, or shrink bands). Aviation involves constant vibration from engines and turbulence — a loosely threaded cap that would survive a smooth highway trip may work itself open in flight.

**Real-World Scenario**: A shipper packages 12 bottles of a Class 8 corrosive liquid in a fiberboard box for air shipment. Each bottle is 500 mL. The shipper uses bubble wrap between the bottles but does not include any absorbent material. At the air freight facility, the cargo is rejected. The shipper must repackage with absorbent pads capable of absorbing at least 6 liters (12 bottles x 500 mL) before the shipment can fly. This delay costs the shipper a day and an additional packaging expense that could have been avoided by doing it right the first time.

## Section 6: Shipper's Declaration for Dangerous Goods

Air shipments of hazardous materials require a specific document called the **Shipper's Declaration for Dangerous Goods**. This is required in addition to any standard shipping papers, bills of lading, or other documentation you might prepare for surface transport. The form is mandated by the ICAO Technical Instructions and the International Air Transport Association (IATA) Dangerous Goods Regulations.

### What Makes This Different from a Highway Shipping Paper

A standard highway shipping paper (bill of lading) includes the proper shipping name, hazard class, UN number, packing group, and quantity. The Shipper's Declaration for Dangerous Goods requires all of that plus:

- The name and address of the shipper and the consignee
- The airport of departure and the airport of destination
- A declaration that the shipment is within the limitations prescribed for the type of aircraft (passenger or cargo only)
- The number and type of packages
- The packing instruction number from the ICAO Technical Instructions
- The authorization (if applicable) for any special permits or exemptions
- A signed certification by the shipper that the contents are properly identified, classified, packaged, marked, labeled, and in proper condition for transport by air

### Two Copies Required

The shipper must provide at least **two signed copies** of the Declaration. One accompanies the shipment; the other is retained by the air carrier at the origin. The document must be in English (additional languages are permitted but English is mandatory for international shipments).

### Common Errors That Get Shipments Rejected

Air cargo facilities check Shipper's Declarations carefully. The most common reasons for rejection include:

- Missing or incorrect packing instruction number
- Quantity exceeds the limit for the declared aircraft type
- Shipper's certification not signed or dated
- Proper shipping name does not match the UN number
- "Cargo Aircraft Only" not declared when required

**Practical Tip**: If your fleet delivers hazmat to an air freight forwarder, confirm that the Shipper's Declaration is in the envelope with the shipment before you leave the origin. A missing Declaration means the air carrier will refuse the freight, and your driver may get a call to return and pick it up.

## Section 7: Lithium Batteries

Lithium batteries deserve their own section because they are the most common hazmat air transport issue facing fleet operators today. They are everywhere — in laptops, cell phones, power tools, electric vehicles, medical devices, and thousands of consumer products — and they are a serious fire risk when damaged, defective, or improperly packaged.

### Why Lithium Batteries Are Dangerous in Air Transport

Lithium batteries can enter "thermal runaway," a self-heating chain reaction that produces intense fire, toxic gases, and projectile fragments. A single battery in thermal runaway can ignite adjacent batteries, creating a cascading fire that overwhelms aircraft fire suppression systems. The halon-based systems in aircraft cargo holds were designed for Class A fires (paper, wood) and are not effective against lithium battery fires.

### Classification Categories

Lithium batteries are divided into two types:
- **Lithium metal batteries** (non-rechargeable) — UN3090 (standalone) or UN3091 (packed with/contained in equipment)
- **Lithium ion batteries** (rechargeable) — UN3480 (standalone) or UN3481 (packed with/contained in equipment)

Each type has different quantity thresholds that determine how restrictive the shipping requirements are.

### Section II vs Section I

The ICAO Technical Instructions divide lithium batteries into two sections based on size:

**Section II** covers smaller batteries (e.g., lithium ion cells up to 20 Wh, batteries up to 100 Wh; lithium metal cells up to 1 g lithium content, batteries up to 2 g). These have less restrictive requirements — they may ship as "excepted" with simplified documentation, limited marking, and no full Shipper's Declaration. Most consumer electronics batteries fall into Section II.

**Section I** covers larger batteries that exceed Section II thresholds. These require full Class 9 hazmat shipping procedures: proper shipping name, UN number, Class 9 label, Shipper's Declaration, and compliance with all air transport packaging requirements.

### Standalone vs Packed With vs Contained In

The regulations distinguish between three configurations:

- **Standalone batteries** (not installed in or packed with any device) — These face the strictest rules. Standalone lithium ion batteries shipped by air must be at a **state of charge not exceeding 30%** of their rated capacity. This requirement exists because a lower state of charge reduces the energy available to feed a thermal runaway event.

- **Packed with equipment** (batteries are in the same package as the device but not installed in it) — Slightly less restrictive. The batteries must be individually protected against short circuits and must be packed so they cannot move within the outer packaging.

- **Contained in equipment** (batteries are installed in the device) — Least restrictive. The device must be packaged to prevent accidental activation during transport, and the battery must be protected against short circuits.

### Recent Regulatory Changes

Regulations around lithium batteries continue to tighten. Key recent developments include:

- Standalone lithium ion batteries (UN3480) are **forbidden on passenger aircraft** as cargo. They may only fly on cargo aircraft. This has been in effect since 2016 and remains in force.
- The 30% state of charge requirement for standalone lithium ion batteries on cargo aircraft.
- Enhanced packaging requirements including fire-resistant containers and new performance testing standards.
- Proposals for active fire suppression systems in aircraft cargo compartments specifically designed for lithium battery fires.

**Real-World Scenario**: Your fleet delivers a pallet of power tool kits to an air freight facility. Each kit contains a cordless drill with a lithium ion battery installed (contained in equipment, UN3481) plus two spare batteries packed alongside (packed with equipment, UN3481). The spare batteries are 40 Wh each — well within Section II limits. The shipment qualifies for Section II exceptions, but each package must be marked with the lithium battery handling mark, and the air waybill must include the statement "Lithium ion batteries in compliance with Section II of PI 966" (for the spares packed with equipment, the applicable packing instruction is PI 967). If the spare batteries were 150 Wh each, they would fall under Section I and require full Class 9 shipping procedures including the Shipper's Declaration. Knowing the watt-hour rating of the batteries before you ship determines which rules apply.

## Section 8: Relevance to Fleet Operators

You might be thinking: "My fleet only runs trucks on highways. Why do I need to know air transport rules?"

The answer is that the transportation chain does not stop at the mode boundary. If your fleet picks up hazmat from a shipper and delivers it to an air freight facility, a seaport, or a rail terminal, the shipment must already comply with the requirements for the next mode. The air carrier will reject a shipment that does not meet air transport standards — and the rejection creates delays, extra costs, and potential violations for everyone in the chain.

Here are the situations where air transport rules directly affect highway fleet operators:

**Delivering to air freight forwarders.** If the cargo is bound for air transport, the shipper must package and document it to air standards. Your driver should verify that the Shipper's Declaration is present, the Cargo Aircraft Only labels are applied (if required), and the packages are marked correctly before accepting the load.

**Picking up air freight for final-mile delivery.** When your driver picks up hazmat at an airport cargo facility for delivery to the consignee, the shipment was packaged and documented for air transport. The highway leg still requires your own shipping papers, placards (if applicable), and compliance with Part 177 highway rules.

**Shipper errors flow downstream.** If a shipper improperly packages a lithium battery shipment for air transport and your fleet delivers it to the airport, the air carrier will reject it. Your driver is now stuck with refused freight, needs to coordinate a return, and your customer is unhappy. Understanding the basics of air transport rules helps your team catch problems before they leave the dock.

**Intermodal awareness.** Many modern supply chains use multiple modes. A shipment may move by truck to the airport, fly to a hub city, move by truck again to a distribution center, and then move by truck once more to the final customer. Compliance must be maintained across every handoff.

## Key Takeaways
- Air transport has the strictest hazmat rules due to altitude pressure changes, temperature extremes, and inability to stop mid-flight
- Column 9A (passenger aircraft) and 9B (cargo aircraft only) in the Hazmat Table determine what can fly and in what quantity
- Packaging for air requires inner containers, absorbent material, and pressure differential testing
- The Shipper's Declaration for Dangerous Goods is mandatory for air hazmat shipments
- Standalone lithium ion batteries are forbidden on passenger aircraft and must be at 30% state of charge or less for cargo aircraft
- Highway fleet operators who deliver to or pick up from air freight facilities must understand air transport standards

### Q1
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: A material has "Forbidden" in Column 9A and "60 L" in Column 9B of the Hazardous Materials Table. What does this mean for air transport?
**A**: The material cannot be transported by air under any circumstances
**B**: The material can only be transported on cargo-only aircraft, up to 60 liters net per package
**C**: The material can be transported on passenger aircraft in quantities up to 60 liters
**D**: The material requires a special permit from PHMSA for any air transport
**Correct Answer**: B
**Explanation**: Column 9A applies to passenger aircraft and Column 9B applies to cargo aircraft only. "Forbidden" in 9A means no passenger flights. A quantity in 9B means the material is allowed on cargo-only aircraft up to that quantity per package. The package must bear a "Cargo Aircraft Only" label.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 175.75

### Q6
**Type**: scenario
**Question**: Your fleet delivers a shipment of corrosive liquid to an air freight forwarder. The air carrier rejects the shipment because the packages do not contain absorbent material and the Shipper's Declaration is missing from the documentation envelope. Who is responsible for correcting these deficiencies?
**A**: The air carrier, because they are the final handler before the flight
**B**: Your fleet, because you were the last highway carrier to handle the shipment

**Correct Answer**: C
**Explanation**: Under the HMR, the shipper (offeror) is responsible for properly classifying, packaging, marking, labeling, and documenting hazardous materials before they are offered for transportation.
