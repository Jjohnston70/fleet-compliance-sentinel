# Shipping Papers

## Learning Objectives
- Explain the purpose of hazmat shipping papers and why they are critical for safety
- List all required information elements on a hazmat shipping paper
- Describe the correct format and sequence for hazmat descriptions
- Explain where shipping papers must be kept in the cab at all times
- Identify the requirements for emergency response telephone numbers
- Understand the shipper's certification and when it is required
- Describe shipping paper retention requirements for shippers and carriers
- Recognize common shipping paper violations

## Section 1: What Are Shipping Papers?

Shipping papers are the written documents that must accompany every shipment of hazardous materials during transportation. They serve two critical purposes: regulatory compliance and emergency response.

For regulatory compliance, shipping papers provide a verifiable record that the hazardous material was properly classified, described, and packaged before being offered for transport. They connect the shipper, the carrier, and the consignee in a documented chain of accountability. If an inspector pulls your truck over and asks what you are hauling, the shipping papers are the official answer.

For emergency response, shipping papers may be the first — and sometimes the only — source of information available to firefighters, hazmat teams, and law enforcement when they arrive at an accident scene involving hazmat. If a driver is incapacitated after a rollover and cannot communicate, responders will look for the shipping papers to identify what materials are involved, how dangerous they are, and whom to call for expert guidance. The information on those papers can determine whether responders approach the scene in standard gear or full chemical suits, whether they evacuate a quarter-mile or a half-mile, and whether they use water or a specialized agent to address a spill or fire.

This is why the location of shipping papers in the cab is strictly regulated. When the driver is at the controls of the vehicle, the shipping papers must be **within the driver's reach while the seat belt is fastened** — typically in a door pocket, a pouch mounted on the door, or clipped to the visor. When the driver is away from the vehicle, the shipping papers must be placed **on the driver's seat** or in a holder mounted on the inside of the driver's door, where they are visible and immediately accessible to anyone who opens the door.

**Why this matters in real life**: A state trooper arrives at a jackknifed tractor-trailer on the highway at 2 AM. There is liquid leaking from the trailer. The driver was taken to the hospital by ambulance. The trooper opens the driver's door and needs to find the shipping papers instantly — not rummage through a messy cab, not dig through a briefcase, not search behind the seats. If the papers are on the driver's seat as required, the trooper can identify the material, call CHEMTREC, and direct the hazmat response team within minutes. If the papers cannot be found, the entire scene becomes exponentially more dangerous and response time increases dramatically.

## Section 2: Required Information

Every hazmat shipping paper must include the following information for each hazardous material in the shipment. Missing any of these elements is a violation.

### The Basic Description (The "Big Four")

The basic description of a hazardous material must include these four elements in a specific sequence:

1. **UN/NA Identification Number**: The four-digit ID number preceded by "UN" or "NA" (e.g., UN1203 for gasoline).

2. **Proper Shipping Name**: The exact name from the Hazardous Materials Table, Column 2 (e.g., "Gasoline"). No trade names, abbreviations, or slang.

3. **Hazard Class or Division Number**: The numeric hazard class from the Hazmat Table, Column 3 (e.g., "3" for flammable liquid).

4. **Packing Group**: Shown in Roman numerals — I, II, or III (e.g., "II" for gasoline). Not all materials have packing groups.

The required sequence for these four elements is: **ID number, proper shipping name, hazard class, packing group**. For example:

> UN1203, Gasoline, 3, PG II

### Additional Required Information

Beyond the basic description, the following must also appear on the shipping paper:

5. **Total Quantity**: The total amount of hazardous material, by weight or volume, including the unit of measurement (e.g., "2,000 gallons" or "500 lbs"). The number and type of packages must also be stated (e.g., "10 drums" or "1 cargo tank").

6. **Number and Type of Packages**: The count and description of packages (e.g., "4 cylinders," "20 pails," "1 portable tank").

7. **Emergency Response Telephone Number**: A telephone number where someone knowledgeable about the specific material can be reached 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. This is covered in detail in Section 4.

8. **Shipper's Certification**: A signed statement certifying that the shipment has been properly classified, described, packaged, marked, labeled, and is in proper condition for transportation. This is covered in Section 5.

### Optional but Common Additional Information

- **Technical name**: Required when the proper shipping name is generic (has a "G" symbol in the Hazmat Table). Must appear in parentheses immediately after the proper shipping name.
- **Subsidiary hazard class**: If the material has more than one hazard, subsidiary hazards must be listed in parentheses after the primary hazard class.
- **Reportable Quantity (RQ)**: If the material is listed as a hazardous substance under CERCLA and the quantity in a single package meets or exceeds the reportable quantity, "RQ" must appear before or after the basic description.
- **"HOT"**: Required for elevated temperature materials transported at or above 212 degrees Fahrenheit (100 degrees Celsius) for liquids.
- **"Marine Pollutant"**: Required if the material meets the marine pollutant criteria.

### Putting It All Together: Example Shipping Paper Entry

Here is what a complete hazmat entry looks like on a shipping paper:

> **HM** | **Description** | **Quantity**
> X | UN1203, Gasoline, 3, PG II | 5,000 gallons, 1 cargo tank

With emergency response phone number at the top or bottom of the document:
> Emergency Contact: CHEMTREC 1-800-424-9300, Contract #12345

And a signed shipper's certification at the bottom.

## Section 3: Format and Order Rules

The HMR imposes strict formatting requirements to ensure that hazmat entries are immediately identifiable on a shipping paper — especially when the paper also lists non-hazardous materials.

### Identifying Hazmat Entries

When a shipping paper lists both hazardous and non-hazardous materials, the hazmat entries must be made clearly distinguishable by one of these three methods:

1. **Listed first**: The hazmat entries appear before any non-hazmat entries on the paper. This is the simplest approach.

2. **Highlighted**: The hazmat entries are highlighted in a contrasting color or with a visible border. The highlighting must be something that would be obvious on a photocopy — do not use a highlighter color that disappears when the paper is copied.

3. **Marked with an "X"**: An "X" is placed in a column designated as "HM" (Hazardous Material) next to each hazmat entry. This column must be clearly labeled so the reader knows what the "X" means.

Many carriers and shippers use a combination of these methods for extra clarity. The goal is simple: anyone looking at the shipping paper should be able to immediately see which entries are hazmat without reading every line.

### Basic Description Sequence

The four elements of the basic description must appear in this order:

1. Identification number
2. Proper shipping name
3. Hazard class or division
4. Packing group

Additional information (total quantity, technical name, subsidiary hazard) may be placed before the basic description, after it, or in separate columns, but the four elements of the basic description must maintain their prescribed sequence.

**Correct**: UN1203, Gasoline, 3, PG II
**Incorrect**: Gasoline, 3, UN1203, PG II (proper shipping name and class cannot precede the ID number)

### Consistency

All hazmat entries on a single shipping paper must use the same format. If you use an HM column with an "X" for one hazmat entry, use it for all of them. If you list hazmat entries first, all hazmat entries must come before all non-hazmat entries — you cannot scatter them throughout the document.

## Section 4: Emergency Response Telephone Number

Under 49 CFR 172.604, every hazmat shipping paper must include a telephone number where someone knowledgeable about the hazardous material being shipped can provide immediate emergency response information 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

### Requirements for the Phone Number

The phone number must meet all of these criteria:

- **Monitored at all times**: The number must be answered 24/7/365. A number that goes to voicemail after business hours does not comply. An automated system that requires a caller to navigate a menu or leave a message does not comply.

- **Knowledgeable person**: The person answering must be able to provide detailed information about the hazardous material, or must have immediate access to someone who can. "I don't know, our hazmat manager comes in at 8 AM" is not acceptable when a firefighter calls at 3 AM.

- **Specific to the material**: The person must be able to provide information about the specific material in the shipment — not just generic hazmat information.

### CHEMTREC

Many shippers and carriers use **CHEMTREC** (Chemical Transportation Emergency Center) to meet this requirement. CHEMTREC is operated by the American Chemistry Council and provides 24/7 emergency response information for hazardous material incidents.

- **CHEMTREC phone number**: 1-800-424-9300
- CHEMTREC requires a contract or registration. The shipper's CHEMTREC contract number should appear on the shipping paper next to the phone number.

Using CHEMTREC is not required — a shipper can use its own 24/7 number if it meets all the regulatory criteria. But for many small and mid-size shippers, CHEMTREC is the most practical way to comply because maintaining a staffed 24/7 phone line is expensive and operationally challenging.

### Where the Phone Number Appears

The emergency response telephone number can appear:

- Next to each hazmat entry on the shipping paper
- At the top or bottom of the shipping paper in a clearly identified location (acceptable if it applies to all hazmat entries on the paper)
- On a separate document that is attached to and accompanies the shipping paper

The number must be clearly associated with the hazmat shipment so that a first responder or inspector does not have to guess which phone number to call.

### Common Phone Number Violations

- **General company number**: A main office line that is not staffed after hours or on weekends.
- **Cell phone of the shipper's contact**: Acceptable only if that person is truly available 24/7 and knowledgeable about the material. In practice, individual cell phones often go unanswered.
- **No number at all**: A surprisingly common violation, especially on shipments prepared by infrequent hazmat shippers.
- **Disconnected or wrong number**: The number must actually work. Inspectors and auditors do test them.

## Section 5: Shipper's Certification

The shipper's certification is a signed statement on the shipping paper that certifies the hazardous material has been properly prepared for transportation. Under 49 CFR 172.204, the certification must read substantially as follows:

> "This is to certify that the above-named materials are properly classified, described, packaged, marked, and labeled, and are in proper condition for transportation according to the applicable regulations of the Department of Transportation."

Alternatively, the shortened form is acceptable:

> "I hereby declare that the contents of this consignment are fully and accurately described above by the proper shipping name, and are classified, packaged, marked and labeled/placarded, and are in all respects in proper condition for transport according to applicable international and national governmental regulations."

### Who Signs

The shipper (or their authorized agent) must sign the certification. The signature can be manual (handwritten) or digital, and must include the signer's name and date.

### When It Is Required

The shipper's certification is required on virtually all hazmat shipping papers. There are limited exceptions — for example, a shipper who is also the carrier does not need to certify to themselves, and certain movements of empty hazmat packagings may be exempt.

### Why It Matters

By signing the certification, the shipper is accepting legal responsibility for the accuracy of everything on the shipping paper and the compliance of the physical shipment. If the material is misclassified, the packaging is wrong, or the labels are missing, the person who signed that certification can be held personally liable — not just the company.

**Practical Tip for Drivers**: Before you pull out of a shipper's facility, check that the shipper's certification is signed. An unsigned certification is a violation that will be attributed to both the shipper and the carrier. It takes two seconds to check and can save thousands in penalties.

## Section 6: Hazardous Waste Manifest

When hazardous materials are also regulated as hazardous waste under EPA rules, a Uniform Hazardous Waste Manifest (EPA Form 8700-22) is used in place of — or in addition to — standard shipping papers.

### Key Differences from Standard Shipping Papers

- **Manifest Tracking Number**: Every hazardous waste manifest has a unique 12-digit tracking number issued by the receiving state. This number follows the waste from the point of generation through transportation to its final treatment, storage, or disposal facility.

- **EPA ID Numbers**: The generator (shipper), each transporter, and the designated receiving facility must have EPA identification numbers. These numbers appear on the manifest.

- **Cradle-to-Grave Tracking**: The manifest creates a chain of custody. The generator prepares the manifest, the transporter signs for receipt, and the receiving facility signs upon delivery and sends a copy back to the generator. If the generator does not receive a signed copy from the receiving facility within 35 days, they must file an Exception Report with the EPA.

- **Multiple Copies**: The manifest is a multi-copy form. Each party in the chain retains copies for their records.

### What Drivers Need to Know

If you are hauling hazardous waste, you must have the hazardous waste manifest in the cab, signed by both the generator and you (the transporter). You must deliver the waste only to the facility designated on the manifest. If you cannot deliver to the designated facility, you must contact the generator for revised instructions — you cannot simply take it to a different facility.

Hazardous waste manifests must be retained by the carrier for at least **3 years** from the date the waste was accepted for transport. This is longer than the standard shipping paper retention period.

## Section 7: Retention Requirements

The HMR requires both shippers and carriers to retain copies of hazmat shipping papers after the shipment is complete.

### Shipper Retention

Under 49 CFR 172.201(e), the shipper must retain a copy of the hazmat shipping paper (or an electronic image of it) for a minimum of **2 years** after the material is accepted by the initial carrier. The retained copy must include the date of acceptance by the carrier.

### Carrier Retention

Under 49 CFR 177.817(f), the carrier must retain a copy of the hazmat shipping paper for a minimum of **1 year** after the material is accepted. The retained copy must include the date of acceptance.

### Hazardous Waste Manifests

As noted above, hazardous waste manifests must be retained for **3 years** from the date the waste was accepted for transport.

### Why Retention Matters

Retained shipping papers serve as evidence of compliance during audits and investigations. If an incident occurs months after a shipment was made — a contamination event, a latent health effect, a delayed complaint — the shipping papers are the primary record of what was shipped, how it was described, and who was responsible. Without retained records, a carrier or shipper has no defense against allegations of non-compliance.

**Practical Tip**: Set up a systematic filing process — by date, by customer, or by material — so that you can retrieve shipping papers quickly when an inspector or auditor requests them. A box of unsorted papers in a back room does not meet the spirit of the regulation, even if technically the papers exist.

## Section 8: Common Violations

Shipping paper violations are among the most frequently cited hazmat infractions during roadside inspections and facility audits. Here are the mistakes that get companies in trouble.

### Violation 1: Missing Emergency Response Phone Number
This is the single most common shipping paper violation. The phone number is missing entirely, or the number listed goes to voicemail, an automated menu, or a person who has no knowledge of the material. Inspectors routinely call the number to verify it works — and they do it during off-hours to test 24/7 availability.

### Violation 2: Wrong Description Sequence
The basic description must follow the prescribed order: ID number, proper shipping name, hazard class, packing group. Rearranging these elements — even if all the correct information is present — is a violation. This trips up shippers who design their own bill of lading forms with columns in the wrong order.

### Violation 3: Unsigned Shipper's Certification
The shipper's certification must be signed. A printed certification without a signature is incomplete. This is an easy violation to catch and an easy one to prevent — just check before the truck leaves.

### Violation 4: Hazmat Entries Not Distinguished
When a shipping paper lists both hazmat and non-hazmat items, the hazmat entries must be highlighted, listed first, or marked with an "X" in an HM column. If hazmat entries are buried in the middle of a long list of non-hazmat items with no visual distinction, that is a violation.

### Violation 5: Missing or Wrong Information
Common errors include: wrong UN/NA number, wrong hazard class, missing packing group, incorrect proper shipping name (using a trade name), missing quantity, or missing technical name when required for a generic (n.o.s.) entry.

### Violation 6: Papers Not Accessible in the Cab
The shipping papers must be within the driver's reach while belted or on the driver's seat when the driver is away from the vehicle. Papers stuffed in the glove compartment, behind the seat, or in a backpack on the sleeper berth do not comply.

### Violation 7: Outdated or Retained Papers for Previous Loads
Keeping old shipping papers from previous hazmat loads in the cab creates confusion. If an inspector or first responder finds shipping papers for materials that are no longer on the vehicle, they may respond to the wrong hazard. Remove shipping papers from the cab promptly when a hazmat load is delivered. Filed copies belong in the office, not in the truck.

---

## Key Takeaways
- Shipping papers must accompany every hazmat shipment and be immediately accessible in the cab
- The basic description sequence is: ID number, proper shipping name, hazard class, packing group
- The emergency response phone number must connect to a knowledgeable person 24/7
- The shipper's certification must be signed before the vehicle leaves the facility
- Shippers retain shipping papers for 2 years; carriers retain them for 1 year
- Hazmat entries must be visually distinguished from non-hazmat entries on the shipping paper
- Shipping papers on the driver's seat when the driver is away — within arm's reach when the driver is belted in

## Assessment Questions

### Q1
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: When a driver leaves the cab of a vehicle transporting hazardous materials, where must the shipping papers be placed?
**A**: In the glove compartment
**B**: On the driver's seat or in a holder mounted on the inside of the driver's door
**C**: In the sleeper berth
**D**: In a sealed envelope on the dashboard
**Correct Answer**: B
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 177.817(e), when the driver is not at the vehicle controls, the shipping papers must be placed on the driver's seat or in a holder mounted to the inside of the driver's door, where they are visible and immediately accessible to emergency responders or inspectors.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 177.817(e)

### Q2
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: What is the correct sequence for the basic description of a hazardous material on a shipping paper?
**A**: Proper shipping name, hazard class, ID number, packing group
**B**: Hazard class, proper shipping name, packing group, ID number
**C**: ID number, proper shipping name, hazard class, packing group
**D**: Packing group, ID number, hazard class, proper shipping name
**Correct Answer**: C
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 172.202(a), the basic description must appear in this sequence: identification number, proper shipping name, hazard class or division, and packing group. Placing these elements out of order is a violation, even if all the required information is present on the document.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.202(a)

### Q3
**Type**: true_false
**Question**: The emergency response telephone number on a hazmat shipping paper can be a company number that goes to voicemail after business hours, as long as the voicemail greeting includes emergency instructions.
**Correct Answer**: False
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 172.604, the emergency response telephone number must be monitored at all times — 24 hours a day, 7 days a week — by a person who is knowledgeable about the hazardous material being shipped or who has immediate access to such a person. A voicemail system does not meet this requirement, regardless of the greeting content.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.604

### Q4
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: How long must a motor carrier retain copies of hazmat shipping papers after accepting a hazardous materials shipment?
**A**: 6 months
**B**: 1 year
**C**: 2 years
**D**: 3 years
**Correct Answer**: B
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 177.817(f), highway carriers must retain a copy of hazmat shipping papers for at least 1 year after the material was accepted for transport. Shippers have a longer retention requirement of 2 years. Hazardous waste manifests require 3-year retention.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 177.817(f)

### Q5
**Type**: scenario
**Question**: You are reviewing a shipping paper for a load you are about to accept. The paper lists: "Gasoline, UN1203, 3, PG II, 5,000 gallons, 1 cargo tank." The emergency response phone number is listed as CHEMTREC 1-800-424-9300, and the shipper's certification is printed at the bottom but has no signature. Should you accept this shipment?
**A**: Yes — all the required information is present
**B**: No — the basic description is in the wrong sequence
**C**: No — the shipper's certification is unsigned, and the basic description sequence is wrong
**D**: No — CHEMTREC cannot be used as an emergency response number
**Correct Answer**: C
**Explanation**: There are two problems. First, the basic description is out of sequence — it should read "UN1203, Gasoline, 3, PG II" (ID number first, then proper shipping name), not "Gasoline, UN1203, 3, PG II." Second, the shipper's certification must be signed under 49 CFR 172.204. An unsigned certification is incomplete. The driver should request corrections before accepting the shipment. CHEMTREC is a valid emergency response number, so that is not the issue.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.202(a), 49 CFR 172.204

### Q6
**Type**: multiple_choice
**Question**: When a shipping paper lists both hazardous and non-hazardous materials, which of the following is an acceptable method for identifying the hazmat entries?
**A**: Printing the hazmat entries in a smaller font size
**B**: Placing the hazmat entries on a separate page stapled to the back
**C**: Entering an "X" in a column marked "HM" next to each hazmat entry
**D**: Writing "DANGER" in red ink next to each hazmat entry
**Correct Answer**: C
**Explanation**: Under 49 CFR 172.201(a)(1), hazmat entries must be distinguished from non-hazmat entries by one of three methods: listing them first, highlighting them, or placing an "X" in a column marked "HM." Using smaller font, separate pages, or writing "DANGER" are not recognized methods under the regulation.
**CFR Reference**: 49 CFR 172.201(a)(1)
