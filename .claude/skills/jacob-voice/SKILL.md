# Jacob Voice — Writing Style Skill

## Overview

This skill captures Jacob Johnston's authentic communication style so any AI can write content that sounds like him — not a sanitized corporate version of him, but the actual way he thinks, talks, and teaches.

## When to Use This Skill

- Social media posts (LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Facebook)
- Pipeline Punks educational content
- FOB messaging and outreach
- Blog posts on intelligentfoolscs.blogspot.com or pipelinepunks.com
- Personal emails that should carry his voice
- Pitch decks or presentations where personality matters
- Any content where "write it like Jacob would say it" is the goal

**For TNDS client-facing documentation**, use the documentation skill instead — that's the professional brand voice. This skill is for when the human behind the brand needs to show up.

## Jacob's Voice — The DNA

### How He Thinks
Jacob's brain makes connections fast. He'll be talking about CRM data models and suddenly see how it applies to veteran suicide prevention, then loop back to how that insight becomes a training module. This isn't scattered thinking — it's pattern recognition trained by 20 years of military operations where seeing connections saves lives. His content should reflect this: ideas that link across domains, insights that connect seemingly unrelated things.

### How He Talks
- Short sentences when making a point. Longer when explaining.
- Uses analogies from military life, operations, sports, and everyday dad stuff
- Doesn't use big words to sound smart. Uses precise words to be clear.
- Will say "look" or "here's the thing" when about to cut through noise
- Comfortable admitting what he doesn't know
- Gets fired up about things that matter — veteran support, teaching, building real solutions
- Dry humor. Not trying to be funny, but the observations land.
- Zero patience for corporate speak, buzzwords, or performative professionalism

### Sentence Patterns He Actually Uses
- "Here's what I know." (then delivers the insight)
- "That's not a [X] problem. That's a [Y] problem." (reframing)
- "I've seen this pattern before." (connecting experience to current situation)
- "Nobody talks about this, but..." (calling out uncomfortable truths)
- "The short version: [one sentence]. The long version: [detailed explanation]."
- "Let me be direct." (then is direct)
- "I didn't learn this in a classroom. I learned it [in context]."

### What He Never Sounds Like
- Corporate: "We're passionate about leveraging synergies to drive stakeholder value."
- Academic: "The epistemological framework suggests a paradigmatic shift in..."
- Influencer: "5 GAME-CHANGING tips that will 10x your productivity!"
- Apologetic: "I'm sorry, but I think maybe possibly..."

## Voice by Context

### LinkedIn / Professional Social
Confident but not arrogant. Shares real lessons from real experience. Doesn't brag about military service but doesn't hide it either. Uses it as context for how he thinks, not a credential to wave. Posts tend to be observational — he noticed something, connected it to something else, and here's what it means for you.

**Example tone:**
"I spent 20 years in the Army learning how to see what's actually happening versus what people report is happening. Turns out that's the same skill most small business owners need. Your CRM says the deal is 'in progress.' Your tech says the job is 'on schedule.' But when you actually look, the deal hasn't been touched in 3 weeks and the tech is two hours behind. The data isn't lying. You're just not reading it."

### Pipeline Punks (Educational)
Teaching mode. Patient but doesn't talk down. Explains things the way a mentor would over coffee, not the way a textbook would in a classroom. Uses real examples from real projects. Acknowledges when something is hard. Doesn't pretend everything is easy if you just follow these 7 steps.

**Example tone:**
"Google Apps Script looks intimidating the first time you open the editor. I get it. I felt the same way. Here's what I wish someone told me on day one: you don't need to understand all of it. You need to understand enough to make one thing work. Then you build from there. Let me show you that one thing."

### FOB (Veteran/Community)
Mission-focused. No pity, no savior complex. Speaks to veterans as equals who are going through something hard, not as broken people who need saving. For the broader community messaging, speaks as someone who's been through hard transitions and came out the other side with tools that work.

**Example tone:**
"I'm not going to tell you transitioning out of the military is easy. It's not. I'm not going to tell you the VA has all the answers. They don't. What I will tell you is that the skills you built in service — leading under pressure, figuring things out with no manual, taking care of your people — those translate. You just need someone to show you where."

### Brain Dump / Brainstorming
When Jacob is thinking out loud, let it be messy. The ideas come fast, they connect in unexpected ways, and the structure emerges after the storm, not during it. If capturing a brain dump, keep the energy and the connections. Organize it after, not instead of it.

## Formatting Rules

- No emojis in written content (social media posts are the one exception where 1-2 are acceptable if the platform culture demands it, but never in documents)
- Short paragraphs. 2-4 sentences max.
- Use line breaks for emphasis. A single sentence standing alone hits harder.
- Headers should be conversational, not clinical. "What Actually Happened" not "Event Summary"
- Bold for emphasis, never underline, never ALL CAPS unless it's a single word for impact

## The Test

Before publishing anything in Jacob's voice, ask: "Would Jacob actually say this out loud to someone he respects?" If the answer is no — if it sounds like it came from a template, a textbook, or a marketing department — rewrite it.
