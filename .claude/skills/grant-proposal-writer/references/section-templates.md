## Abstract / Executive Summary {#abstract}

**Write this LAST** even though it appears first in the proposal.

### Structure (typically 1 page or 250 words)
```
[Organization name] requests [$amount] from [funder] to [verb: implement/expand/launch]
[program name], a [brief description] serving [specific population] in [geographic area].

[1-2 sentences on the need with one key data point]

[1-2 sentences on the approach — what the program does, operationally]

[1-2 sentences on expected outcomes with specific numbers]

The [X-month/year] project will [key deliverable] and [key outcome], advancing
[funder's stated priority area].
```

### What Reviewers Look For
- Can they understand the entire proposal in 60 seconds?
- Is the request amount and population clear?
- Does it connect to the funder's priorities?

### Common Mistakes
- Writing it first (before the rest is clear)
- Making it too vague ("serve the community" instead of specifics)
- Forgetting to state the dollar amount requested
- Not mentioning the funder's priorities

## Need / Problem Statement {#need}

The need statement answers: "Why should this funder invest money in solving this problem?"

### Structure
```
Opening Hook: [Most compelling local data point or brief scenario]

National Context: [1-2 sentences with national statistics showing the scope]

State/Regional Context: [Narrow to state-level data]

Local Context: [Narrow to the specific community — this is where reviewers pay most attention]

Target Population: [Specific demographics, geographic area, characteristics]
- Who exactly will be served?
- How many people are affected?
- What makes this population underserved or at-risk?

Root Causes: [What's driving the problem — not just symptoms]

Current Gaps: [What's missing in the current service landscape?
Why existing services aren't sufficient?
What would change if this problem were addressed?]

Connection to Funder: [Explicit connection to what the funder has said they care about]

Transition to Solution: [1-2 sentences bridging to the program design section]
```

### Data Layering Pattern
```
National:  "Across the United States, [X statistic from federal source]."
State:     "In Colorado, [Y statistic from state agency]."
Local:     "In El Paso County specifically, [Z statistic from local data]."
Population: "Among [target population], [specific statistic]."
Gap:       "Despite these numbers, [what's missing from current services]."
```

### What Reviewers Look For
- Data from credible, recent sources (within 3-5 years)
- Local data (not just national trends applied locally)
- Clear connection between the problem and the proposed solution
- Understanding of root causes, not just symptoms
- Acknowledgment of community strengths (not deficit-only framing)
- Evidence the applicant knows the community and its needs

### Common Mistakes
- Only using national statistics without local context
- Circular reasoning ("we need this program because we don't have it")
- Describing the problem so broadly it could apply anywhere
- Not connecting the need to the specific funder's priorities
- Using outdated data without justification
- Only describing deficits without acknowledging community assets

### Strong Need Statement Signals
- Cites CDC, Census, state agency, and local sources
- Includes demographics of target population (age, geography, income, race/ethnicity)
- Names the specific community or region
- Explains why existing services are insufficient
- Uses the funder's own language to describe the problem

## Program Design / Project Narrative {#design}

This section answers: "What will you actually do, and why will it work?"

### Structure
```
Program Overview: [2-3 sentence summary of the approach]

Theoretical Framework / Evidence Base:
- What research or evidence supports this approach?
- What models or best practices does the program draw from?
- If the approach is new, what's the theoretical basis?

Logic Model: [Table or visual — see logic-model-guide.md]

Activities (in detail for each major activity):
  Activity 1: [Name]
  - What: [Specific description of the activity]
  - Who delivers it: [Staff role and qualifications]
  - Who receives it: [Target participants, eligibility, recruitment]
  - When/How often: [Schedule, frequency, duration]
  - Where: [Location/platform]
  - Dosage: [How much service per participant]
  - Expected output: [What this activity produces]
  - Connected outcome: [What change this activity contributes to]

  Activity 2: [Name]
  ... [same structure]

Recruitment and Retention:
- How will participants be identified and recruited?
- What barriers to participation exist and how will they be addressed?
- What strategies will maintain engagement?

Staffing Plan:
- Key personnel with roles and qualifications
- FTE allocations
- Supervision structure
- Training plan

Timeline: [Table format]
  | Month/Quarter | Activity | Milestone | Responsible |
  |--------------|----------|-----------|-------------|
  | ...          | ...      | ...       | ...         |

Partnerships:
- Named partners with specific roles
- Evidence of commitment (MOUs, letters of support)
- How partnerships strengthen the program
```

### What Reviewers Look For
- Operational detail: could someone actually run this program from the description?
- Clear connection between activities and outcomes (logic model)
- Evidence-based or evidence-informed approach
- Realistic timeline and staffing
- Attention to recruitment and retention (not assuming participants appear)
- Partnerships that are genuine (not just letter-of-support-collectors)

### Common Mistakes
- Describing goals instead of activities
- Vague language: "provide mentorship" without describing what mentorship looks like operationally
- No dosage information (how much service does each person receive?)
- Timeline that's unrealistic for the budget and staffing
- Activities that don't connect to stated outcomes
- Key personnel described without relevant qualifications
- Partnerships mentioned without specific roles

## Organizational Capacity {#capacity}

This section answers: "Can you actually pull this off?"

### Structure
```
Organization Overview:
- Mission and founding (brief — 2-3 sentences max)
- Years of operation
- Annual budget size (shows scale of operations)
- Service area and populations served

Relevant Experience:
- Prior programs similar to what's being proposed
- Prior grants managed successfully (amounts, funders, outcomes achieved)
- Experience serving the target population
- Specific results from past work (with numbers)

Key Personnel:
  [Name], [Title] — [X% FTE on this project]
  [2-3 sentences: relevant education, experience, and accomplishments
  that qualify them for this specific role]

  [Name], [Title] — [X% FTE on this project]
  ...

Infrastructure:
- Facilities and equipment
- Technology systems
- Financial management capacity (accounting systems, audit history)
- Data collection and reporting systems

Partnerships and Collaboration:
- Named partners with specific roles in this project
- History of collaboration
- MOUs or formal agreements (reference attached documents)

Board Governance:
- Board composition and relevant expertise
- Board engagement in this program
- Oversight and accountability structure

[If newer organization:]
Capacity Building:
- How the organization will build capacity during the project
- External support and technical assistance plans
- Board development plans
- Financial management growth strategy
```

### What Reviewers Look For
- Specific evidence of ability to deliver (not just claims of expertise)
- Key personnel with credentials that match their proposed roles
- Prior grant management experience (for federal grants especially)
- Financial management capacity proportional to the request amount
- Active, engaged board (for nonprofit applicants)

### Common Mistakes
- Spending a full page on organizational history instead of relevant capacity
- Listing staff without connecting their qualifications to the proposed project
- No mention of financial management systems
- Claiming partnerships without evidence (no MOUs or letters)
- Not addressing capacity gaps honestly (reviewers will find them anyway)

## Evaluation Plan {#evaluation}

This section answers: "How will you know if it worked, and what will you do with that information?"

### Structure
```
Evaluation Overview:
- Purpose of evaluation (formative and summative)
- Who will conduct the evaluation (internal, external, or mixed)

Outcomes and Indicators:
  | Outcome | Indicator | Target | Data Source | Collection Schedule |
  |---------|-----------|--------|-------------|-------------------|
  | [What changes] | [How measured] | [Specific number] | [Tool/method] | [When] |
  | ...     | ...       | ...    | ...         | ...               |

Important: Distinguish outputs (what you produce) from outcomes (what changes).
  - Output: "120 mentoring sessions delivered"
  - Outcome: "75% of participants report increased sense of belonging"

Process Evaluation:
- Are activities being implemented as planned?
- Are we reaching the intended population?
- What implementation challenges arise and how are they addressed?
- Participant satisfaction and feedback mechanisms

Outcome Evaluation:
- Pre/post measures for participant-level change
- Comparison to baseline data
- Validated instruments used (name specific tools)
- Follow-up data collection timeline

Data Collection Methods:
- Surveys (which instruments, validated or custom)
- Administrative data (program records, attendance)
- Interviews or focus groups (with whom, how often)
- Existing data sources (school records, agency data)

Data Analysis Plan:
- How quantitative data will be analyzed
- How qualitative data will be coded and interpreted
- How findings will be reported

Use of Findings:
- How evaluation data will inform program improvement (feedback loop)
- How findings will be shared with stakeholders
- How data will be used for sustainability planning

[For federal grants, add:]
Agency-Specific Measures:
- GPRA measures (SAMHSA)
- Common Measures (DOL)
- VA-specific reporting metrics
```

### What Reviewers Look For
- Specific, measurable outcomes (not vague aspirations)
- Realistic targets with justification
- Named data collection instruments (not "we will use surveys")
- Clear feedback loop (data → analysis → program improvement)
- Appropriate evaluation design for the program and budget

### Common Mistakes
- Confusing outputs with outcomes
- No baseline (how do you measure change without a starting point?)
- Aspirational targets without basis ("100% of participants will...")
- No data collection timeline or responsibility
- Evaluation budget insufficient for what's described
- No plan for how evaluation findings will actually be used

## Sustainability Plan {#sustainability}

This section answers: "What happens when this grant ends?"

### Structure
```
Sustainability Vision:
- What does the program look like in 3-5 years?
- Which components continue, which are one-time investments?

Revenue Diversification (specific strategies):
1. [Source]: [Description, timeline, estimated amount]
2. [Source]: [Description, timeline, estimated amount]
3. ...

Good sources to consider:
- Additional grants (different funders, not just "reapply")
- Fee-for-service revenue
- Membership or subscription models
- Corporate partnerships
- Individual giving/fundraising
- Government contracts
- Social enterprise revenue
- In-kind support from partners

Institutionalization:
- How will program elements become embedded in ongoing operations?
- What partnerships will sustain the work?
- What community ownership has been built?

Cost Reduction Over Time:
- Which startup costs won't recur?
- How will efficiency improve after the initial investment?
- What volunteer capacity can replace paid positions?

Sustainability Timeline:
  | Year | Grant Funding | Other Revenue | Total | Notes |
  |------|--------------|---------------|-------|-------|
  | 1    | $XX,XXX      | $X,XXX        | $XX,XXX | Launch year |
  | 2    | $XX,XXX      | $XX,XXX       | $XX,XXX | Diversifying |
  | 3    | $0           | $XX,XXX       | $XX,XXX | Self-sustaining |
```

### What Reviewers Look For
- Specific revenue sources (not just "we will seek additional funding")
- Realistic timeline for diversification
- Evidence of planning (not afterthought)
- Community buy-in that supports long-term viability

### Common Mistakes
- "We will apply for more grants" as the entire sustainability plan
- No specific dollar amounts or revenue projections
- No timeline for when other revenue kicks in
- Ignoring the question entirely or providing a one-sentence answer
- Claiming self-sufficiency without a plausible path
