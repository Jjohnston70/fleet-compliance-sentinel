# Logic Model Building Guide

How to construct a logic model that connects program activities to measurable outcomes. This is the backbone of a fundable proposal.

---

## What Is a Logic Model

A logic model is a visual representation of the causal chain between what you invest, what you do, and what changes as a result. It answers: "If we do X, then Y will happen, because Z."

Most funders expect some version of this chain:

```
INPUTS → ACTIVITIES → OUTPUTS → SHORT-TERM OUTCOMES → LONG-TERM OUTCOMES
```

---

## The Five Components

### 1. Inputs (Resources)
What you invest to run the program.
- Funding (this grant + other sources)
- Staff time and expertise
- Facilities and equipment
- Partnerships and community relationships
- Participant time and engagement
- Data and research

### 2. Activities
What the program does with those inputs.
- Direct services (mentoring sessions, workshops, counseling)
- Training (staff training, peer training, participant skill-building)
- Outreach and recruitment
- Partnership development
- Data collection and evaluation

### 3. Outputs (Deliverables)
The direct products of activities — what you count.
- Number of participants served
- Number of sessions/workshops delivered
- Number of referrals made
- Materials produced
- Staff trained

**Key distinction:** Outputs measure what you DID. Outcomes measure what CHANGED.

### 4. Short-Term Outcomes (1-12 months)
Changes in knowledge, attitudes, skills, or awareness.
- Increased knowledge of [topic]
- Improved skills in [area]
- Changed attitudes toward [behavior]
- Increased awareness of [resources]
- New connections formed

### 5. Long-Term Outcomes (1-3+ years)
Changes in behavior, condition, or status.
- Changed behavior (employment, help-seeking, reduced risk)
- Improved conditions (housing stability, mental health, academic performance)
- Systems change (policy, community infrastructure, service integration)
- Population-level impact (reduced rates of [problem])

---

## Logic Model Table Format

Use this format when a visual diagram isn't practical:

```
| Inputs | Activities | Outputs | Short-Term Outcomes | Long-Term Outcomes |
|--------|-----------|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| [Resource] | [Activity] | [Count] | [Knowledge/skill change] | [Behavior/condition change] |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
```

### Example: Veteran Mentorship Program

```
| Inputs | Activities | Outputs | Short-Term Outcomes (6 mo) | Long-Term Outcomes (1-3 yr) |
|--------|-----------|---------|---------------------------|----------------------------|
| Grant funding ($50K) | Recruit and train veteran mentors | 15 trained mentors | Mentors report increased sense of purpose (80%) | Reduced veteran suicide risk indicators |
| Staff (1.5 FTE) | Match mentors with youth participants | 30 mentor-youth pairs | Youth report having a trusted adult (85%) | Youth demonstrate improved academic/career outcomes |
| SMB partner network | Weekly mentoring sessions | 480 mentoring sessions/year | Youth report increased career awareness (75%) | Veterans employed in civilian careers (70%) |
| Training curriculum | SMB apprenticeship placements | 10 apprenticeship placements | Veterans report reduced isolation (80%) | Sustained mentoring relationships beyond program |
| Community partnerships | Crisis response training (Buddy Backup) | 15 crisis-trained responders | Participants know how to access crisis support (95%) | Community-level crisis response capacity |
| Evaluation tools | Data collection and program evaluation | Quarterly evaluation reports | Program improvements based on data feedback | Evidence base for program expansion |
```

## Building the Logic Model: Step by Step

### Step 1: Start with the End
What long-term outcomes does the funder want to see? Work backwards from there.

### Step 2: Define Short-Term Outcomes
What needs to change first (knowledge, skills, attitudes) before behavior changes?

### Step 3: Design Activities
What activities will produce those short-term changes? Base on evidence or theory.

### Step 4: Specify Outputs
What will you count to know activities are being delivered?

### Step 5: Identify Inputs
What resources are needed to execute the activities?

### Step 6: Check the Chain
For each connection, ask: "Is it reasonable to expect that [this activity] will lead to [this outcome]? What's the evidence?"

---

## Common Logic Model Mistakes

**Broken chain:** An outcome listed that no activity supports. ("Improved community health" when the program only serves 30 individuals.)

**Missing middle:** Activities jump straight to long-term outcomes without short-term changes. ("Mentoring sessions → reduced suicide" skips the intermediate steps.)

**Outputs as outcomes:** "500 participants served" is an output, not an outcome. What changed for those 500 people?

**Too ambitious:** Claiming population-level impact from a small program. Be honest about the scale of change your program can realistically achieve.

**No evidence connection:** Activities chosen without any basis in research or practice evidence.

## Assumptions and External Factors

Strong logic models also acknowledge:

**Assumptions** — What must be true for the model to work?
- Participants will engage consistently
- Partners will fulfill their commitments
- Staff can be recruited with needed qualifications
- Community conditions remain relatively stable

**External Factors** — What could affect outcomes that's outside your control?
- Economic conditions
- Policy changes
- Other programs starting or stopping
- Community crises or events

Including these shows sophistication and honesty. Funders prefer applicants who have thought about what could go wrong, not just what will go right.
